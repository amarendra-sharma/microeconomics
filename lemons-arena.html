Lemons Market Arena

Watch a market where nobody lies destroy itself anyway: slide the share of hidden lemons past the tipping point and see the good cars flee; run an insurance pool through its death spiral round by round; then install the fixes real markets invented — and learn to spot all four information diseases in the wild.

Chapter 16's markets fail for a new reason: one side knows something the other cannot see. This arena runs the chapter's used-car market. Sellers know their car; buyers know only the odds. A peach is worth $8 to a buyer and $7 to its owner; a lemon is worth $3 to a buyer and $2 to its owner — so every car, honestly traded at the right price, creates value. Whether those trades survive depends on one number: λ, the share of lemons hiding in the pool. The Info Detective record saves for reporting back.

1The unraveling slider

Buyers can't tell peach from lemon, so they will pay only the average: price = 8(1−λ) + 3λ = 8 − 5λ. But peach owners won't part with a $7 car for less than $7 — and there is the trap. Slide λ and watch for the exact moment the market snaps.

⚠ Nobody lied

Read the wreckage carefully: no seller misrepresented anything, no buyer was cheated, every actor priced rationally — and the peaches vanished anyway, taking their $1-per-car gains from trade with them. Adverse selection is not fraud; it is the anticipation of hidden quality poisoning honest trades. That is why disclosure laws alone can't fix it and why Station 3's mechanisms had to be invented.

2The insurance death spiral

Now the same disease in its most consequential host. Five residents want health coverage; each knows their own expected claims, the insurer knows only the pool's average. Each round, the insurer prices at the current pool's average cost — and everyone who values coverage less than the price walks. Press the button and watch selection do its work.

The spiral stopped — but count the wreckage: only the two costliest residents hold coverage, at a premium near the top of the range, while three people who each valued insurance above its true cost to provide went bare. Every exit made the pool sicker and the price higher, chasing out the next-healthiest — selection eating the market from the bottom up. This is why real systems fight the spiral with mandates, open-enrollment windows, employer pools, and subsidies: not to help insurers, but to hold the healthy in the pool so the market can exist at all.

✦ Sharma's Take — half the institutions around you are Akerlof repairs

Once you have watched the peaches flee, you cannot unsee the machinery civilization built to bring them back. Warranties, professional licenses, brand names, dealer certifications, credit scores, references, probation periods, diplomas, deductibles, wedding rings — each one is an answer to the question this arena kept asking: how do you trade with someone who knows more than you? The deep move in every fix is the same, and it is worth stating once cleanly: make honesty cheaper than deception. A warranty is cheap for a peach owner and ruinous for a lemon's; a deductible is tolerable for the careful and expensive for the reckless; four years of a demanding degree is a bearable cost precisely for the people it certifies. The signal works because faking it costs the faker more.

And the death spiral deserves permanent shelf space in your mind, because it is the rare piece of microeconomics that decides elections. Whenever you hear "mandate," "open enrollment," "pre-existing condition," or "community rating," you are hearing policymakers wrestle with Station 2 — trying to keep the $3-value resident in the same pool as the $11 one so that a market in promises can exist. Information problems are not a footnote to supply and demand; for insurance, credit, labor, and used anything, they are the market design problem.

— A.S.
