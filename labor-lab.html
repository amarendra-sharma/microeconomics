Labor Market Lab

Run the hiring desk with both dials — the wage and the price of what workers make; set a minimum wage in a competitive market and count the queue; then visit the company town, where a single employer bends the rules and a wage floor does something the competitive model says is impossible.

Chapter 14 turned the market diagram around: firms buy, households sell, and the "price" is the wage. This lab runs the chapter's numbers — Mara's crew adding 12, 10, 8, 6, 4, 2 cups per successive hire, and the city market Ld: w = 24 − 0.12L, Ls: w = 4 + 0.08L, clearing at $12 and 100 baristas — and then goes one station past the chapter, into monopsony. ⚠ Both effects are real at once

A binding floor in this competitive market does two things simultaneously: the baristas who keep their jobs genuinely earn more, and the market genuinely employs fewer of them — while the higher wage draws even more people into the search. The winners are visible on every payslip; the losers are a queue that never formed into jobs. Honest analysis holds both in view — and then asks the question Station 3 was built for: is the market actually competitive?

3The company town — beyond the chapter

Now travel to a mining town with one employer. This station goes a step past Chapter 14, and it is worth the trip. The mine faces the whole town's labor supply, so hiring one more worker means raising the wage for everyone — its true marginal cost of labor (MCL) climbs twice as fast as the supply curve. The mine hires where MCL meets the workers' value (MRP), then pays only what the supply curve requires: 60 workers at $10, each producing $16 of value. A competitive market here would hire 80 at $12. Now do the forbidden experiment: impose a minimum wage on the monopsonist.

Walk the slider slowly and watch employment trace an inverted U: 60 with no floor, rising as the floor climbs to $12 — peaking at the full competitive 80 — then falling back as the floor overshoots. Under monopsony, a well-placed wage floor doesn't fight the market; it disarms the employer's wage-setting power, because a mine that must pay $12 regardless no longer saves money by keeping its workforce small. The competitive prediction and this one differ in sign — which converts the minimum-wage debate from a theorem into an empirical question about how much power employers actually hold.

✦ Sharma's Take — the model you argue with is doing its finest work

Station 3 is the most important thing in this arena, and not because it tells you whether to support minimum wages — it doesn't. It tells you what the disagreement is about. In the competitive market of Station 2, a floor must cost jobs; in the company town, the same floor can create them. Both are impeccable logic from different premises, which means the loudest question in labor-market politics is secretly an empirical one: how much wage-setting power do employers really have? That is precisely the question modern labor economics spends its energy measuring — and why careful studies of minimum-wage hikes find small effects in some markets and real losses in others. When two smart people disagree about the minimum wage, they are usually disagreeing about Station 2 versus Station 3 without knowing it. Now you know it.

And keep the detective's discipline for every wage that offends or impresses you: run the chapter's four forces in order. Most differentials fall to the first three — skill, unpleasantness, scarcity — and the benchmark's power is that whatever those three cannot explain is exactly where to aim the harder questions. Wages track the value of the marginal hour; the exceptions are not embarrassments to the theory but its most useful output.
