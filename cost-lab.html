Cost Curve Lab

Watch cost curves get manufactured — from workers' shrinking marginal product, through the full U-shaped anatomy, to an experiment the chapter can only describe: shock the rent and see which curves flinch. Then prove you can tell the four curves apart blindfolded.

Chapter 9 makes one claim above all: cost curves are not drawn, they are produced — every dollar figure downstream of a production fact. This lab runs Mara's cart on the chapter's exact numbers: the worker schedule where marginal product rises through the third hire and then diminishes, and the smooth cost family TC = 48 + 3.6q − 0.12q² + 0.006q³ whose AVC bottoms at $3 at 10 cups and ATC at $6 at 20 cups. 1The productivity–cost mirror

Every worker at Mara's cart costs the same $12 a day — but the cups they add differ, so the cost of a cup differs by who brewed it. The marginal cost of output produced by worker L is simply the wage divided by that worker's marginal product, MC = w / MP. Slide through the hires and watch the two charts move as mirror images: whenever the next worker adds more, the next cup costs less.

⚠ Why MC crosses the averages at their minimums — the GPA rule

No geometry is needed, only the logic of averages. Your next exam grade (the marginal) pulls your GPA (the average) toward itself: below the average it drags it down, above it pushes it up. So an average can only stop falling at the exact moment the marginal climbs through it — which is why MC pierces AVC at $3 and ATC at $6, precisely at their bottoms, always and in every firm.

3The rent shock — which curves flinch?

An experiment the textbook can only narrate, run live. Mara's landlord changes the cart's daily rent — the fixed cost, $48 in the base case. Predict before you slide: which of the four curves will move? The gray dashed ghosts are the base-rent curves, so every twitch is visible. What moves — and what conspicuously refuses to — is the chapter's deepest managerial lesson.

⚙ Shock the fixed cost

Slide the rent. Watch who reacts.

MC and AVC never moved — rent appears in neither, because it does not change with output. Only ATC (and its fixed slice, AFC) shifted, dragging the break-even point with it. The consequence, coming due in Chapter 10: since the firm's supply behavior is built from MC and the AVC shutdown floor, a rent change alters Mara's profit but not a single one of her short-run production decisions. Rent is the textbook's favorite sunk cost, and the curves just showed you why sunk means ignorable.

✦ Sharma's Take — cost curves are a firm's biography, and MC is the only chapter that decides

Here is the reading I want you to leave with. AFC is the firm's history — commitments already made, spread ever thinner. ATC is its accounting — what the accountant reports at year's end. But MC is its present tense: the only number that answers "should we make one more?" Station 3 was the proof. The rent tripled, the accountant's curves jumped, the profit line moved — and the production decision did not budge, because the decision never consulted anything but MC and the shutdown floor. Most costly business mistakes are category errors between these chapters of the biography: pricing off average cost, chasing volume to "spread the overhead," staying open to "cover the rent."

And keep the mirror from Station 1 in your pocket, because it turns cost data into a diagnostic. Whenever you see a firm's marginal cost curve bend upward, you are looking at diminishing returns to some fixed factor — a crowded kitchen, a saturated server, a full truck. Rising MC is not a pricing choice; it is the shadow of a production constraint. Find the constraint, and you have found both the reason costs rise and the most valuable thing to fix.
