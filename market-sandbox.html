Market Sandbox

The coffee market of Verenia's capital, alive on one page. Shift the curves, fire real-world shocks, post your own price and watch the market fight back — then prove you can read a market's fingerprints in the Shock Detective.

This arena runs the exact market from Chapter 4 of the textbook: demand Qd = 100 − 20P, supply Qs = 40P − 20, which settle at $2.00 and 60 cups when nothing disturbs them. Nothing here is animation for its own sake — every line you see is computed live from those two equations, and everything you learned in the chapter can be tested against it. Work through the three stations in order, and report your Shock Detective score back to your instructor at the end.

⚠ The distinction this page enforces — movement vs. shift

Notice what the sliders above cannot do: change coffee's own price. That is deliberate. A change in the good's own price never moves a curve — it slides the market along fixed curves. The curves themselves move only when something else changes: incomes, tastes, input costs, the number of sellers. Keep that straight and half of Chapter 4's exam questions solve themselves.

2Post a price — and let the market push back

Now play price-setter in the original market (demand and supply at their starting positions). Post any price you like and see how many cups buyers want versus how many vendors bring. Whenever those two numbers disagree, the market is not at rest — press Let the market react and watch the price get pushed, one step at a time, toward the only price where the gap closes.

3Shock Detective

Here is the skill that separates people who have read Chapter 4 from people who can use it. Something hits Verenia's coffee market overnight. You never see the curves — only the news and the before-and-after numbers, exactly like reading real market data. Your job: name which curve moved, and which way. The tell is the fingerprint you met in Station 1 — price and quantity moving the same direction points at demand; opposite directions point at supply.

✦ Sharma's Take — this is how economists actually read the news

Nobody ever hands a working economist the curves. What arrives is what the Shock Detective hands you: a headline and two numbers that moved. Oil is up and consumption is down — supply story. House prices and sales volumes climbing together — demand story. The fingerprint rule you have been drilling is not a classroom trick; it is the first diagnostic any of us runs on real data, and it will quietly upgrade how you read every business story from now on.

One honest caveat before it becomes a reflex: the rule assumes one curve moved. When both shift at once — a pandemic that cuts supply and demand together — one of the two movements becomes ambiguous, and you need more evidence to untangle it. Chapter 4 flags this, and the later chapters give you the tools. But one-shock diagnosis is the foundation, and after twenty cases here you will own it.

— A.S.
