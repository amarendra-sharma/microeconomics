Consumer Choice Sandbox

Fill a real basket under a real budget and hunt the utility maximum with your own hands; watch the budget line and indifference curves find each other; then do what the whole chapter was building toward — construct a demand curve out of nothing but choices.

Chapter 8 answers the question the earlier chapters postponed: where does the demand curve come from? The answer is built in three moves — a budget that limits, satisfaction that diminishes, and a rule that balances the last dollar — and this sandbox makes you perform each one. Station 1 is the chapter's opening problem with the training wheels off. Stations 2 and 3 run the smooth geometry of budget lines and indifference curves. Station 4 is the Basket Check, the graded game whose record saves for reporting back.

1Fill the basket

Mara has $18 for her morning round: coffee at $2 a cup, pastries at $3 each. Later units please less than earlier ones — her exact satisfaction schedules are the chapter's. In the book, a slider spent the whole budget for you. Here nobody helps: add and remove items freely, overspending is simply impossible, and underspending is legal but costly. Hunt the best basket; the meter remembers your record.

⚠ The rule is per dollar, not per unit

The most common Chapter 8 error is comparing raw marginal utilities. A pastry's last unit may deliver more utils than a coffee's — and still be the worse buy, because it costs half again as much. Divide by the price first, always: the currency of choice is utility per dollar. That single division is why cheap pleasures crowd expensive ones out of an optimal basket more than raw enjoyment alone would suggest.

2The tangency machine

Section 8.4 replaced counted utils with drawn preferences: indifference curves, each connecting bundles the consumer likes equally, climbing in satisfaction away from the origin. The consumer's problem becomes geometric — reach the highest curve the budget line touches — and the answer is always a tangency. Here is a consumer with smooth, even-handed preferences over coffee and pastries. Move her income and the price of coffee; the machine recomputes her best bundle live. Watch two things: how the line moves (parallel for income, pivot for price) and how the tangency point slides along with it.

⚙ Budget line meets indifference map

Shift the line; the optimum follows

At every tangency you just produced, the same condition held: the slope of the indifference curve (the marginal rate of substitution — the consumer's own exchange rate between the goods) equals the slope of the budget line (the market's exchange rate, the price ratio). MRS = Px/Py is the equimarginal rule of Station 1 wearing geometry: two roads, one optimum.

3Build a demand curve from choices alone

Now the chapter's finale. Fix income at $18 and pastries at $3, and let only the price of coffee move. Every price you visit poses the full choice problem; the consumer solves it; and her chosen coffee quantity at that price is one point on her demand curve. Visit at least five different prices and the curve you have been drawing since Chapter 4 will assemble itself in the lower chart — not assumed, derived.

✦ Sharma's Take — you already run this algorithm; the chapter just names it

Students meet "utils" and suspect economists of inventing a fake substance. Fair — but watch what you did in Station 1. Nobody handed you utility numbers in a grocery store, yet you constantly perform the comparison they formalize: is the last dollar here worth more than the last dollar there? Streaming subscriptions you trimmed, the third coffee you skipped, the upgrade you decided wasn't worth it — each was an equimarginal judgment. The model doesn't claim you compute; it claims your choices behave as if you do, and that "as if" is what lets one curve summarize a million shoppers.

And Station 3 is the chapter's quiet triumph, so don't rush past what happened there. The demand curve you traced was not assumed, surveyed, or decreed — it precipitated out of one consumer's repeated best choices as a single price moved. Every demand curve you have drawn since Chapter 4, and every one you will draw after, is exactly that: the visible trail of invisible optimizing. When a curve is built from a mechanism rather than sketched from habit, you know precisely when to trust it — and what has to change for it to move.

— A.S.
