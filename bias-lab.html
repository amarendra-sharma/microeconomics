Bias Lab

Every other arena tested a market. This one tests you. Four classic experiments from the behavioral-economics literature, run on a subject pool of one — then a detective round to spot the same biases loose in the world. Answer honestly; the lab only works on the truth.

⚠ Knowing the bias is not immunity

The uncomfortable finding of this literature: experts exhibit these biases too, at reduced but nonzero strength — anchoring works on judges setting sentences and realtors pricing houses after they've been taught about anchoring. The lab's goal is not to cure you; it is to teach you to build guardrails: decide your maximum price before hearing the asking price, automate the saving before the paycheck arrives, reframe every "70% survive" as "30% die" and see if your choice holds. Rationality, in practice, is mostly good architecture.

✦ Sharma's Take — the calculator was a portrait of nobody, and a benchmark for everybody

Students sometimes finish this chapter believing behavioral economics demolished the sixteen chapters before it. Look more carefully at what you just did. Every experiment in this lab measured your behavior against the rational benchmark — the anchor's pull is the distance from the unanchored estimate, your λ is a departure from the λ = 1 of expected value, the framing flip is a violation only because the rational choice is frame-proof. Without homo economicus there is no yardstick, and without a yardstick there are no findings. Behavioral economics did not burn down the building; it installed better instruments in it.

And keep the practical asymmetry: markets punish some biases and prey on others. Arbitrageurs profit by correcting mispricings, which disciplines prices — but casinos, subscription trials that bill after you forget, "was $499, now $299" tags, and payday lenders are all business models built on the stations you just walked through. The chapter's deepest lesson is therefore self-defense: know which number anchored you, ask what the loss frame is hiding, and never negotiate with your own present self without a commitment device in hand. You now own both toolkits — the map of how people should choose, and the map of how they do. The economist's craft is knowing which map the moment calls for.

— A.S.
