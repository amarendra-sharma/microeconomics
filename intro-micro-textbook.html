Chapter 1 · Part I: Foundations

The Economic Way of Thinking

Six recurring themes that organize how microeconomists reason — scarcity, incentives, gains from trade, markets and their failures, thinking at the margin, and the rules of the game — introduced not as settled rules but as questions the discipline keeps returning to

This book is set in Verenia, a mid-sized economy within the MacroNations universe, and we will remain there throughout. In the macro simulation, a nation is governed from above, through choices such as tax and interest rates. Here we adopt the opposite vantage point and examine the economy from the inside — a single coffee cart in the capital, a nearby bakery, a farm at the edge of town, and the people who buy from all three. Microeconomics is the study of these individual decisions, and of how millions of them, with no one coordinating, combine to produce the prices, quantities, and living standards of an entire nation.

Before we define a single term, I want you to make a decision.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

Mara has one hour. What should she make?

Notice you cannot have more of one without giving up some of the other. The curve is a wall: every point on it is a real choice, everything beyond it is impossible this hour, and everything inside it wastes time. You just drew Mara's production possibilities frontier — and the rate at which coffee trades for pastries along it is the single most important idea in this chapter.

That slider illustrates the foundation of microeconomics. Mara cannot make more coffee without making fewer pastries; the pastries she gives up to brew one more cup are the opportunity cost of that cup. This single trade-off recurs throughout the book in progressively more complex forms.

✦ Sharma's Take — why I don't teach "the ten principles"

Most introductory books open with a tidy list of ten or so "principles of economics." Those lists are not wrong, exactly, but they are misleading. They hand you a smoothed-over version of the field in which all the hard edges have been filed off and every real debate has quietly been settled in favor of one worldview — roughly: markets work, government usually doesn't, prices are wise. The actual discipline is far more contested than that, and pretending otherwise would be teaching you something we know isn't quite true.

So instead of principles, I organize this book around six recurring themes — questions and patterns the field keeps circling back to, not closed conclusions. Each has a clean textbook version and a messier reality that working economists actually argue about. If you finish understanding both, you will have learned what an introductory course can honestly deliver.

— A.S.

1.1  Theme One — Scarcity forces choice

Here is the starting point of the whole field: human wants exceed available resources, so choices must be made. Mara has one hour, not three. You have one evening, and you cannot both study and see friends with the same hour. Verenia has a fixed stock of workers, land, and machines this year, and a worker building houses is not also growing food. Every economic system ever tried — market, planned, traditional — exists to answer the same unavoidable questions: what gets produced, how, and who gets it. They just answer differently.

The portable idea hiding inside scarcity is opportunity cost: the value of the best thing you gave up to get what you chose. It is portable because it applies to literally every choice. The cost of an extra cup of coffee is the pastries forgone. The cost of an hour studying is the hour of rest or pay you skipped. The cost of Verenia funding a new road is whatever else that budget could have built.

Opportunity cost

The value of the next-best alternative you give up when you make a choice. Not the money you spend — the best thing you could otherwise have done.

In the real world

The true cost of a year of university is not only tuition. It also includes the salary the student could have earned by working that year — often the larger figure of the two. A program that looks affordable on its price tag alone can be expensive once the forgone earnings are counted. This is precisely why economists measure cost by what is given up, not merely by what is paid.

On Mara's frontier the opportunity cost has an exact size. If shifting her whole hour from pastries to coffee turns 12 pastries into 24 cups, then each cup costs her half a pastry. Written as the slope of the frontier,

\( \text{opportunity cost of 1 cup} = \dfrac{\text{pastries given up}}{\text{cups gained}} = \dfrac{\Delta\,\text{pastries}}{\Delta\,\text{cups}} \)

That ratio — what one thing costs in terms of another — is the number markets are quietly computing all the time, as we will see.

⚠ Common pitfall — "I can't afford it"

People say "I can't afford that" as if affordability were a hard wall. Usually it isn't. If you have any money or time at all, you can afford almost any single thing — the real question is what you would have to give up to get it. "I can't afford the trip" almost always means "I'd rather keep the money for other things," which is a choice, not a constraint.

Train yourself to translate: whenever you hear "we can't afford it," swap in "we'd rather use those resources elsewhere," and check whether the argument still sounds the same. Often it doesn't — and that gap is exactly where the economics lives.

Reading a frontier: efficient, wasteful, impossible

Because the frontier marks the limit of what is possible, every production plan falls into one of three categories, shown in the figure below. A point on the frontier (A) is efficient: every resource is in use, so more of one good requires less of the other. A point inside the frontier (B) is attainable but wasteful — some resources sit idle, and more of both goods could be obtained at once. A point outside the frontier (C) is unattainable with current resources.

Figure 1.1 — Points on, inside, and outside a frontier
In the real world

A recession is, in effect, an economy operating at a point inside its frontier — like point B. Factories run below capacity and willing workers cannot find jobs, so the economy produces less than its resources allow. A recovery is the process of returning to the frontier, where those idle resources are put back to work.

Why real frontiers usually bow outward

Mara's frontier is a straight line, which assumes her time is equally suited to coffee and pastries. Real frontiers usually bow outward instead, because resources are not equally good at everything. As production shifts toward more of one good, it must draw in resources progressively less suited to making it, so each additional unit costs more of the other good than the last. This rising sacrifice as one specializes is called increasing opportunity cost, and it is what makes the curve steepen as it descends.

Figure 1.2 — A bowed frontier and increasing opportunity cost
In the real world

Consider a nation shifting resources from farming to manufacturing. The first factories employ workers and land poorly suited to agriculture, so little food is given up. As manufacturing expands, however, it must draw in the best farmland and most capable farmers, and the food sacrificed for each additional factory grows steeply. The frontier bows precisely because the most suitable resources are used first.

1.2  Theme Two — Incentives matter (and often backfire)

People respond to incentives. Raise the price of something and they buy less of it; pay more for work and more work shows up. This is one of the best-supported claims in all of economics: across thousands of studies, demand curves slope down and supply curves slope up. When an economist predicts that a tax on sugary drinks will cut sugary-drink sales, they are just applying this theme.

But the interesting half of the theme is the second half: incentives often produce consequences nobody intended, because people respond to the actual incentive you created, not the one you meant to create. A famous example: when a set of daycares started fining parents for late pickups, late pickups went up. The fine was small, and it quietly converted a social obligation ("don't make the staff wait") into a price ("lateness costs a few dollars, and I'll gladly pay"). The incentive worked — it just worked in the wrong direction.

In Verenia

The capital offers Mara's neighborhood a subsidy for every pastry sold, hoping to support small carts. Mara responds — but so does every other vendor, who all tilt toward pastries and away from coffee. Within a month the morning market is drowning in pastries nobody wants and short on coffee everyone does. The subsidy "worked": it changed behavior. It just didn't produce the outcome the planners pictured.

In the real world

The economists Uri Gneezy and Aldo Rustichini studied a set of daycare centers that began fining parents for picking their children up late. Late pickups did not fall — they rose. The small fine had quietly converted a moral obligation ("do not impose on the staff") into a mere price ("lateness costs a few dollars, and I will gladly pay it"). The incentive changed behavior, just not in the direction intended.

1.3  Theme Three — Trade and specialization create value

If Mara is better at coffee and her neighbor Tomas is better at pastries, both end up with more by specializing and trading than by each doing a bit of everything. Nothing was manufactured in that swap — no new beans, no new flour — yet both are better off. That is the quietly radical claim at the center of economics: trade can create value out of nothing but rearrangement. We will make this precise in Chapter 3, where it turns out the gains depend not on who is better in absolute terms, but on who gives up less — opportunity cost again, wearing a new hat.

1.4  Theme Four — Markets coordinate, and sometimes fail

No one in Verenia's capital is in charge of breakfast. No official decides how many cups of coffee the city needs, or tells Mara what to charge. And yet most mornings there is roughly enough coffee, at a price most people will pay, with very little waste. Prices do the coordinating — they carry information about what is scarce and what is wanted, and they nudge thousands of strangers into rough agreement without any of them intending to cooperate. Much of this book is about how that works.

The honest other half of the theme: markets sometimes fail. When Mara's cart smokes up the whole street, when information is hidden, when one seller corners the market — the price stops telling the truth, and the outcome stops being good. Recognizing when markets coordinate beautifully and when they break is most of the practical skill of microeconomics, and it occupies the back half of this book.

1.5  Theme Five — Rational choice happens at the margin

Big decisions are rare; small adjustments are constant. Mara almost never asks "coffee or pastries, all or nothing?" She asks "should I brew one more cup?" The economist's answer is a rule you will use in every chapter: do it if the extra benefit of that one more unit beats the extra cost. Compare the marginal benefit to the marginal cost, and ignore everything already spent.

Worked example — thinking at the margin
Mara already paid 40 for today's supplies (gone, no refund). A customer offers her 3 for a cup that costs her 1 in beans and time to make. The morning has been slow and she's discouraged about the 40. Should she make the cup?
1
Ignore the 40. It is a sunk cost — already spent, unrecoverable, and identical whether or not she makes this cup. It cannot change a forward-looking decision.
2
Compare only what changes at the margin: the extra revenue (3) against the extra cost (1).
3
3 > 1, so the cup adds 2 to her day. Make it — the gloom about the 40 is real, but irrelevant to this choice.

The principle generalizes into the central rule of rational choice. For any activity, continue as long as the marginal benefit — the extra benefit from one more unit — exceeds the marginal cost — the extra cost of that unit — and stop at the quantity where the two are equal. That balancing point is the optimum, shown where the curves cross below.

Figure 1.3 — Choosing at the margin: benefit versus cost
Marginal benefit and marginal cost

Marginal benefit is the additional benefit from one more unit of an activity; marginal cost is the additional cost of that unit. Net benefit is greatest at the quantity where marginal benefit equals marginal cost.

In the real world

Consider how many hours to study for an exam. The first hour may raise the expected grade considerably; the fifth, much less, as fatigue sets in and the easy gains are exhausted — marginal benefit falls. Meanwhile each additional hour costs more in lost rest and forgone alternatives — marginal cost rises. The sensible stopping point is not "study as much as possible," but the hour at which the next hour's benefit no longer exceeds its cost.

1.6  Theme Six — The rules of the game shape the outcome

The same people, with the same resources, get very different results depending on the rules they operate under. Whether Mara's recipe can be copied freely or is protected, whether contracts are enforced, whether a regulator caps her price — these institutions, the formal and informal rules of the game, quietly decide what behavior pays off. Two neighborhoods identical in every physical way can thrive or stagnate purely on their rules. We will keep returning to this theme, because in microeconomics the "rules" are often a policy lever someone can actually pull.

Six themes, then: scarcity, incentives, trade, markets, the margin, and the rules. Everything ahead is these six, applied to ever-sharper questions — starting in the next chapter with how economists turn fuzzy arguments into models you can actually test.

Thinking Like an Economist

Economics is less a body of facts than a way of reasoning — building deliberately simplified models, separating what is from what ought to be, and refusing to mistake "these two things move together" for "one causes the other"

In Chapter 1 you met the six themes that organize the field. This chapter is about the method — how an economist actually reasons about a claim, including the claims in this book. The single most valuable habit you can build is a kind of disciplined skepticism: not cynicism, but the reflex to ask "how do we know that?" before nodding along. We will start, as usual, with a decision.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

Does selling more iced coffee cause more lemonade sales?

You now run Mara's coffee cart and the lemonade stand beside it. Across a season, the two sell in lockstep — good coffee days are good lemonade days. A friend says: "Push iced coffee hard; it drags lemonade up with it." Study the daily sales and make the call. Then press the button.

The two truly move together — but neither moves the other. A hidden third thing, the day's temperature, pushes both up at once. Brewing extra coffee on a cool day would sell exactly zero extra lemonade. That gap — between things that travel together and things that cause each other — is what this chapter trains you to see.

2.1  Models are deliberate simplifications — that is the point

An economist's main tool is a model: a stripped-down picture of reality that keeps only the few things that matter for the question at hand and throws everything else away. Mara's frontier in Chapter 1 was a model. It ignored the weather, her mood, the brand of her espresso machine, and a thousand other true facts — on purpose. A map that showed every pebble would be useless; its usefulness comes precisely from what it leaves out.

This trips people up. They hear an economist assume "Mara cares only about profit" and object, correctly, that real people are more complicated. But the assumption is not a claim that Mara is a robot. It is a simplification, kept only as long as it helps us predict and discarded the moment it misleads. The right question about an assumption is never "is it literally true?" — it never is — but "does it help us see the thing we are trying to understand?"

Model

A deliberately simplified description of reality — often a diagram, a table, or a few equations — that keeps the forces relevant to a question and ignores the rest, so we can reason and predict.

2.2  What is versus what ought to be

Two very different kinds of claims constantly get tangled together, and separating them is half of thinking clearly. A positive statement is about what is — a description of the world that evidence could, in principle, confirm or refute. A normative statement is about what ought to be — a value judgment that no amount of data can settle on its own.

"A cap on Mara's coffee price would lead her to brew less" is positive: we could gather evidence and check. "Verenia should cap coffee prices so mornings stay affordable" is normative: it rests on what we value, not just on what would happen. Most heated policy arguments are a messy braid of both, and the first move of an economist is to pull the two strands apart — because they are settled in completely different ways.

Positive vs. normative

A positive claim describes what is and can be tested against evidence. A normative claim asserts what ought to be and depends on values. Data can resolve the first; only argument about goals can resolve the second.

2.3  "They move together" is not "one causes the other"

We saw this with the coffee and lemonade. Two variables are correlated when they tend to move together — when high values of one accompany high (or low) values of the other. Correlation is a statement about pattern alone; it says nothing, by itself, about whether either variable causes the other. The scatter below shows a positive correlation: the points trend upward, yet the picture alone cannot tell us why.

Figure 2.1 — What a positive correlation looks like

When two correlated variables are not directly linked, there are three usual culprits, and an economist checks for all three before accepting any causal story.

Reverse causation

The arrow may point the other way. "Neighborhoods with more street vendors have bigger crowds, so vendors must create the crowds." More likely the crowds came first and the vendors set up where the people already were. Plausible stories often run backwards.

A lurking third variable

Something you weren't looking at moves both. That is the coffee-and-lemonade trap exactly: temperature drove both, so they tracked each other while neither touched the other. The lurking variable is the most common reason a confident causal story falls apart.

In the real world

Across the calendar, ice cream sales and drownings rise and fall together. No sensible person concludes that ice cream causes drowning. A lurking third variable — summer heat — drives both: warm weather sends people both to the ice cream stand and into the water. The correlation is real; the causal story is not.

Figure 2.2 — A confounder, drawn as a causal diagram

Drawing the suspected relationships as arrows makes a confounder easy to recognize: a single variable with arrows pointing to both of the others, and no real arrow between them. Temperature drives both ice cream sales and drownings, manufacturing a correlation between two things that never touch.

Selection

The people or cases that "chose" the thing were different to begin with. "Carts that hire a second worker earn more, so hiring causes higher earnings." But the carts that can afford a second worker were already the busy, profitable ones. The gap reflects who selected in, not just the effect of hiring.

⚠ Common pitfall — "Studies show that…"

You will constantly hear "studies show that X causes Y." Most studies show that X and Y correlate — a much weaker thing. Before you believe a causal claim, run the checklist: (1) Could the arrow point the other way? (2) Is some third thing driving both? (3) Were the cases that got X already different? (4) And the master question that contains the rest — compared to what?

2.4  The most useful question an economist asks: "compared to what?"

Every causal claim hides a comparison. To say the tram "caused" a cart's boom is to compare what happened to what would have happened otherwise — the counterfactual. The whole difficulty is that we never get to see the otherwise. So the economist's craft is finding an honest stand-in for it.

Counterfactual

What would have happened to the same people or cases in the absence of the thing whose effect you are measuring. It is never directly observed, which is why a good comparison group is so valuable.

Worked example — did the tram cause the boom?
Carts beside Verenia's new tram stop sell 30% more than they did a year ago. A vendor concludes the tram caused it. Is he right?
1
Name the counterfactual. The honest question is: what would those same carts have sold this year if the tram had never opened? The "30% more than last year" comparison quietly assumes the answer is "the same as last year" — which is exactly what we should doubt.
2
Look for what else changed. The tram was built where the capital was already growing; the whole city's sales may be up this year; new flats opened nearby regardless of the tram. Any of these could lift sales with no help from the tram.
3
Build a stand-in for the counterfactual. Compare the change at tram-stop carts to the change at similar carts far from any tram over the same year. If tram carts rose 30% but comparable carts rose 25% anyway, the tram's real effect is about 5 points, not 30. That "compared to a similar group" move — before-and-after, treated versus untreated — is the workhorse of real-world policy evaluation.

This comparison — tracking a treated group and a similar untreated group before and after — is worth seeing as a picture. The control group's path estimates what the treated group would have done anyway; the gap that opens between them is the effect we can credibly attribute to the change.

Figure 2.3 — A before-and-after comparison with a control group
In the real world

Both towns began at the same level of cart sales. Afterward, the tram town rose by 30 while the comparable town rose by 15 on its own. The honest estimate of the tram's effect is the difference of the differences — roughly 15, not the full 30 — because about half the gain would have appeared even without the tram. This "compared to a similar group" method is the workhorse of credible policy evaluation.

2.5  Why economists disagree

If economics has a method, why do economists so often disagree? Three reasons, worth keeping separate. Sometimes they hold different estimates of the same effect — the evidence is genuinely noisy and reasonable people read it differently. Sometimes they lean on different models that stress different forces. And sometimes — this is the deep one — they agree completely on what a policy would do and still disagree on whether to do it, because they weight competing goals differently. That last disagreement is normative, and no new data will resolve it. A great deal of public argument would clear up instantly if people noticed they were having a values fight, not a facts fight.

✦ Sharma's Take — read every claim with the dial set to "skeptical, not cynical"

Around the 1990s, economics went through a quiet revolution in honesty. The field got much better at separating real causes from comfortable correlations — chasing natural experiments and clean comparisons instead of running a regression and calling it a day. The consequence for you is humbling: a fair number of "facts" confidently taught a generation ago turned out to be poorly identified, and some hard-won findings only became trustworthy once the methods improved.

So I want you reading everything — the news, a politician, this textbook — with one question loaded and ready: compared to what? It is the most powerful three words in the discipline. Not because you should disbelieve everything, but because knowing how a claim was established is the difference between economic reasoning and a good story told confidently.

— A.S.

Trade & Comparative Advantage

How two people — or two nations — can both end up with more simply by specializing and trading, why the one who is better at everything still gains, and why those real gains are shared so unevenly that trade stays politically fraught

This is the first big result of the course, and it is genuinely surprising: two people can each end up with more of everything without anyone working harder or any new resources appearing — purely by rearranging who does what. The engine behind it is the idea you already met in Chapter 1, opportunity cost, now doing the heavy lifting. As always, decide first.

3.1  Better at everything — and still worth trading

Two ideas get confused constantly, so let us pin them down. You have an absolute advantage at a task if you can simply produce more of it than someone else. You have a comparative advantage if you give up less to produce it — if your opportunity cost is lower. These are not the same, and the surprise of this chapter is that comparative advantage, not absolute, decides who should make what.

Put numbers on Mara and Tomas. In a day, Mara can make 24 cups of coffee or 12 pastries; Tomas can make 8 cups or 16 pastries. Mara is better at coffee in absolute terms (24 versus 8). But look at what each gives up:

The bars show absolute advantage at a glance: Mara can make more coffee, Tomas more pastries. But absolute advantage is not what determines who should specialize. For that we compare opportunity costs — the table's last two columns — as the next paragraph does.

To make a cup, Mara gives up only 0.5 of a pastry; Tomas gives up 2. Mara's opportunity cost of coffee is lower, so Mara has the comparative advantage in coffee. Flip it for pastries: Tomas gives up only half a cup per pastry, Mara gives up two — so Tomas has the comparative advantage in pastries. Each should lean into the good they sacrifice least to make.

In the real world

Consider a surgeon who also happens to be the fastest typist in her clinic — an absolute advantage at both tasks. She still hires an assistant to do the typing, because every hour she spends at the keyboard is an hour not spent in surgery, where her advantage is far larger and far better paid. Her comparative advantage, not her raw skill, dictates how she should spend her time. The same logic explains why highly productive countries still import goods they could make perfectly well themselves.

Absolute vs. comparative advantage

Absolute advantage: producing more of a good than someone else can. Comparative advantage: producing it at a lower opportunity cost — giving up less of other things. Specialization should follow comparative advantage, even when one party holds the absolute advantage in everything.

3.2  Where the gains actually come from

Nothing is invented in trade, so where do the extra goods come from? From not wasting effort on your weak task. When each person pours their whole day into the good they are comparatively best at, the same hours produce more in total.

Worked example — the same two people, more for both
Mara and Tomas each work one day. Compare splitting the day evenly (no trade) against full specialization plus a trade at 1 pastry per cup.
1
On their own (each splits the day in half). Mara: 12 cups + 6 pastries. Tomas: 4 cups + 8 pastries. Together the two produce 16 cups and 14 pastries.
2
Each specializes. Mara makes only coffee: 24 cups. Tomas makes only pastries: 16 pastries. Together: 24 cups and 16 pastries — 8 more cups and 2 more pastries from the very same day's work.
3
Trade at 1 pastry per cup. Mara sells Tomas 8 cups for 8 pastries. Mara ends with 16 cups + 8 pastries (she had 12 + 6 alone). Tomas ends with 8 cups + 8 pastries (he had 4 + 8). Both have more than they could make alone — that surplus is the gain from trade.
Figure 3.2 — The combined frontier expands with specialization

Plotting the two producers together makes the gain visible. The frontier above traces every combination the pair can produce jointly. It has a kink because efficiency requires assigning the lower-opportunity-cost coffee producer (Mara) to coffee first; only once she is fully occupied does Tomas, the costlier coffee producer, switch over. The red point marks what the two make by splitting their days evenly without trading — comfortably inside the joint frontier. Specialization moves them out to its edge.

A useful way to see it: trade is a kind of technology. Mara can turn coffee into pastries two ways — by baking (giving up 2 cups per pastry) or by selling coffee to Tomas (giving up far less). When the second route is cheaper, she "produces" pastries more efficiently through trade than she ever could at her own oven.

3.3  Seeing it on the frontier

The same argument can be seen geometrically. Each person's production frontier from Chapter 1 is a straight line whose steepness is their opportunity cost. Work through the diagram one step at a time; the final step presents the entire argument in a single image.

The decisive step is the last. After trading, each person consumes at a point that lies beyond their own frontier — a combination neither could have produced alone. Trade added no resources; by allowing each to specialize and exchange, it expanded the set of outcomes each can attain.

3.4  The price has to land in a band

The deal only works at the right price — the terms of trade. You found the band yourself at the top of the chapter. Mara will not sell a cup for less than the 0.5 pastry she could get by baking; Tomas will not pay more than the 2 pastries it would cost him to brew his own. So any price strictly between 0.5 and 2 pastries per cup leaves both better off. Where exactly it lands inside that band is a matter of bargaining — and it decides how the gains are split.

Terms of trade

The rate at which the two goods are exchanged. Trade benefits both parties only when the terms fall between their two opportunity costs; the exact point within that range determines who captures more of the gain.

⚠ Common pitfall — "They're better at everything, we can't compete"

It feels obvious: if a rival town's bakers are faster at both coffee and pastries, surely Verenia's vendors are doomed. But "faster at everything" is absolute advantage, and that is not what decides trade. As long as the two sides give things up at different rates — different opportunity costs — each still has something it is comparatively best at, and both still gain by specializing.

The same error shows up as "their wages are too low, we can't match them." Lower pay usually reflects lower productivity, not an unbeatable edge. The useful question is never "how do we match their prices on everything," but "what are we comparatively best at — and how do we help the people in the trades we should let go?"

3.5  The half the clean story leaves out

Everything so far is true and is the part most textbooks stop at. But stopping there leaves you unable to explain the world. When Verenia's capital opens trade with a neighboring town, the nation as a whole ends up richer — and yet some specific people are made worse off. The cart that had a comfortable local monopoly on pastries now faces a cheaper rival and loses its niche. The aggregate gain is real; so is that concrete, concentrated loss.

Two facts sit in tension and both are true: the winners gain more than the losers lose (so the gains are real and could, in principle, be shared), and yet that sharing rarely actually happens — the winners almost never write a check to the losers. That gap is most of why trade is so politically charged everywhere, despite economists' near-consensus that it raises total output.

✦ Sharma's Take — hold both halves at once

If you learn Ricardo's argument and stop, you have the toolkit of 1817: you can prove the aggregate gains and you will be baffled by every modern argument against trade. The fuller picture keeps two things in view at the same time — the gains are real and the losses are real and the losers usually are not compensated. Miss any one of those and you will keep being surprised by politics you should have seen coming.

So when someone tells you trade is simply good, or simply bad, you now know the honest answer is more interesting: it reliably grows the pie, it reliably leaves some people holding a smaller slice, and what we choose to do about those people is a values question that the efficiency argument alone cannot answer.

Supply & Demand

The central machine of microeconomics — how the choices of buyers and sellers, with nobody in charge, settle on a price that clears the market, and how to read a changing world by asking which curve moved

No one sets the price of coffee in Verenia's capital. No official determines that the city requires sixty cups each morning, or that each should cost two coins. Yet on most mornings there is roughly enough coffee, at a price most buyers will pay, with little waste. This chapter concerns the mechanism responsible for that outcome — supply and demand — the most widely used tool in the course. We begin by letting you operate it.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

You set the morning coffee price. Find the one that clears the market.

Suppose you could post a single price for coffee across the capital. Too high and cups sit unsold; too low and the lines never end. Slide the price and watch two numbers: how many cups buyers want, and how many vendors bring. Your goal: make the gap disappear.

At a low price, buyers want more than vendors will bring — a shortage, which pushes the price up. At a high price, vendors bring more than buyers want — a surplus, which pulls it down. Only one price leaves no gap. That self-correcting price is the equilibrium, and the market finds it with nobody steering.

4.1  What a market is

A market is just any arrangement that brings buyers and sellers together — a farmers' stall, a stock exchange, an app. We will mostly picture a competitive market: many buyers, many sellers, a roughly standard product, and no single participant big enough to dictate the price. The capital's coffee trade fits well enough — dozens of carts, hundreds of customers, no one cart able to move the going price on its own. That is exactly the setting where the supply-and-demand model predicts best.

4.2  Demand: buyers step back as the price rises

The quantity demanded is how much buyers are willing and able to buy at a given price. The pattern is one of the most reliable in all of economics: raise the price and the quantity demanded falls. That is the law of demand, and drawn with price up the side and quantity along the bottom it gives a downward-sloping demand curve.

Why downward? Two everyday forces. When coffee gets pricey, some people switch to tea — they substitute. And a higher price leaves every wallet a little thinner, so people buy a bit less of most things. Together they bend demand downward.

Law of demand

Holding other things equal, a higher price reduces the quantity demanded, and a lower price raises it — so the demand curve slopes down.

Figure 4.1 — A demand curve
In the real world

When gasoline prices spike — after a refinery outage or a hurricane disrupts supply — drivers respond just as the curve predicts. They combine errands, carpool, postpone long trips, and, over time, shift toward more fuel-efficient cars. A higher price brings a lower quantity demanded; the downward slope is that behavior drawn as a line.

4.3  Supply: sellers offer more as the price rises

The quantity supplied is how much sellers will offer at a given price. Here the relationship runs the other way: a higher price draws out more cups — existing vendors brew extra, and new vendors decide it is worth setting up. That is the law of supply, and it gives an upward-sloping supply curve. The deep reason is opportunity cost again: making one more cup pulls time and beans away from something else, and only a high enough price makes that sacrifice worth it.

Law of supply

Holding other things equal, a higher price raises the quantity supplied, and a lower price lowers it — so the supply curve slopes up.

Figure 4.2 — A supply curve
In the real world

When the price of crude oil climbs, wells and methods that were unprofitable at low prices become worth operating, and producers bring more oil to market; when the price falls, the costliest of them shut back down. Quantity supplied rises and falls with price — the upward slope — because higher prices justify the higher cost of the next, harder-to-produce unit.

4.4  Equilibrium: where the two meet

Put both curves on one graph and they cross once. That crossing is the equilibrium — the price at which the quantity buyers want exactly equals the quantity sellers bring, so the market clears. You already found it by hand above. Away from it, the market is restless: at a price below equilibrium there is a shortage (lines, bidding wars) that pushes the price up; above it there is a surplus (unsold stock) that drags the price down. The price keeps nudging until the gap is gone.

Equilibrium, shortage, surplus

At the equilibrium price, quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. Below it, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied — a shortage. Above it, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded — a surplus. Both push the price back toward equilibrium.

4.5  Reading the world: which curve moved?

The model earns its keep when something changes. The crucial distinction — and the one students most often blur — is between a movement along a curve and a shift of the whole curve. When coffee's own price changes, we move along a fixed curve (a change in quantity demanded or supplied). When any other factor changes, the entire curve shifts. Those other factors deserve to be named precisely, because correct diagnosis depends on identifying which one is at work.

What shifts the demand curve

Buyers' incomes. For most goods, a rise in income increases demand at every price; such goods are called normal goods. For a minority, higher income reduces demand, as buyers move to preferred alternatives they can now afford; these are inferior goods — a cheap instant coffee may lose customers as wages rise. A general increase in the capital's incomes therefore shifts the demand for ordinary coffee rightward if it is a normal good and leftward if it is inferior.

The prices of related goods. Two goods are substitutes when one can take the place of the other, as tea can for coffee; a rise in the price of tea moves some drinkers to coffee and shifts coffee demand rightward. Two goods are complements when they are consumed together, as coffee and pastries are; a rise in the price of pastries reduces coffee demand and shifts it leftward.

Tastes and expectations. A change in preferences — a new fashion for espresso, or a health report — shifts the curve directly. So do expectations: if buyers anticipate higher prices next month, some bring their purchases forward, raising demand today.

The number of buyers. More buyers in the market, such as an influx of office workers to the district, shift demand rightward; fewer shift it leftward.

Normal vs. inferior goods; substitutes vs. complements

A normal good is one whose demand rises with income; an inferior good is one whose demand falls as income rises. Two goods are substitutes if a rise in the price of one increases demand for the other, and complements if a rise in the price of one decreases demand for the other.

Figure 4.3 — A rightward shift in demand
In the real world

In the weeks around major holidays, far more people want to fly at any given fare, so the demand for air travel shifts rightward. Airlines fill their planes and raise prices: both quantity and price rise together. As the diagram shows, a rightward demand shift moves the equilibrium up and to the right.

What shifts the supply curve

Input prices. An input (or factor of production) is any resource used to make the good — for coffee, the beans, milk, labor, equipment, and energy. When an input becomes more expensive, each cup costs more to produce and supply shifts leftward; cheaper inputs shift it rightward.

Technology. An improvement in the method of production — a faster machine, a more efficient routine — lowers the cost of each unit and shifts supply rightward.

The number of sellers. Additional vendors entering the market shift supply rightward; vendors leaving shift it leftward.

Expectations and policy. Sellers' beliefs about future prices, together with measures such as taxes and subsidies, also move the curve: a per-cup tax raises the cost of selling and shifts supply leftward, whereas a subsidy shifts it rightward.

Input (factor of production)

Any resource used to produce a good or service — for coffee, the beans, milk, labor, equipment, and energy. Input prices are among the principal determinants of supply.

In the real world

Outbreaks of avian influenza have repeatedly forced the culling of millions of laying hens. With far fewer hens, the supply of eggs shifts leftward: prices jump while the quantity sold falls. Price up, quantity down — the curves moving in opposite directions — is the signature of a supply decrease, exactly as in the figure.

⚠ Common pitfall — "the price went up, so demand fell"

Not quite, and the difference matters. If coffee's own price rises and people buy less, demand did not fall — the demand curve sat still while buyers slid up along it. That is a fall in quantity demanded. "Demand fell" should be reserved for when the whole curve shifts left (say, a health scare turns people off coffee). Sloppiness here produces the classic error of drawing a shift when you meant a movement, and it will quietly wreck your analysis.

Supply and demand is the most powerful tool in this book, and you will reach for it constantly — gas prices after a storm, sold-out concert tickets, why housing in thriving cities costs a fortune. Within its assumptions it is rigorous and predictive. The danger is forgetting it has assumptions: well-informed buyers and sellers, easy comparison shopping, near-identical products, prices that adjust freely.

Those assumptions fail, sometimes badly, in exactly the markets people care about most. Patients rarely see healthcare prices before they buy. Labor markets never fully clear — matching a worker to a job takes time, so some unemployment persists even "at equilibrium." Housing prices ride partly on what people expect prices to do next. Online platforms tip toward winner-take-all in ways no nineteenth-century economist imagined. So learn this machine cold — then keep a running list of where it stops describing the world. Elasticity

A single measure — the responsiveness of one quantity to another — that decides who bears a tax, why identical shortages produce wildly different price spikes, why a bumper harvest can ruin farmers, and whether a price cut earns more money or less

During the supply disruptions of the early 2020s, many goods grew scarce at once, yet their prices behaved very differently. Toilet paper briefly vanished from shelves but its price barely moved. Used cars, by contrast, became dramatically more expensive and stayed that way for over a year. Semiconductors and baby formula saw sharp, persistent price jumps. The shortages looked similar; the price responses did not. A single concept explains the difference, and it is the subject of this chapter: elasticity, the measure of how responsive one quantity is to a change in another.

Before defining it, consider the decision a seller actually faces.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

Should Mara raise or cut her coffee price to earn more?

Mara wants to maximize the money she takes in — her total revenue, price times quantity sold. It is not obvious whether a higher price (more per cup) or a lower price (more cups) brings in more. Move the price and watch the quantity, the total revenue, and a number called the elasticity. Find where revenue is largest.

At low prices, raising the price brings in more — buyers barely cut back. At high prices, raising it brings in less — buyers flee. Revenue peaks in the middle, exactly where the responsiveness of quantity to price passes through one. That responsiveness is elasticity, and it governs the whole decision.

5.1  Price elasticity of demand

The price elasticity of demand measures how strongly quantity demanded responds to a change in price. It is defined as the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price.

Because quantity falls when price rises, the figure is technically negative; by convention we report its absolute value, written |E|. To avoid an awkward problem — that the percentage change differs depending on which price you start from — economists use the midpoint formula, which divides each change by the average of the two values rather than the starting value:

\[ |E| = \left|\dfrac{\%\,\Delta Q}{\%\,\Delta P}\right| = \left|\dfrac{(Q_2 - Q_1)\,/\,\tfrac{1}{2}(Q_1 + Q_2)}{(P_2 - P_1)\,/\,\tfrac{1}{2}(P_1 + P_2)}\right| \]

Price elasticity of demand

The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price (reported as an absolute value). The midpoint formula computes each percentage change relative to the average of the two values, so the result is the same in either direction.

Worked example — computing an elasticity
When the price of a cup rises from $4 to $6, quantity demanded falls from 120 to 80. What is the price elasticity of demand?
1
Percentage change in quantity. The change is 80 − 120 = −40; the average quantity is (120 + 80)/2 = 100. So %ΔQ = −40/100 = −40%.
2
Percentage change in price. The change is 6 − 4 = 2; the average price is (4 + 6)/2 = 5. So %ΔP = 2/5 = 40%.
3
Divide and take the absolute value. |E| = |−40% / 40%| = 1.0. Quantity changed in exactly the same proportion as price — the boundary case, unit elastic.

5.2  Elastic, inelastic, and unit elastic

The size of |E| sorts demand into three cases. Demand is elastic when |E| > 1: quantity changes proportionally more than price, so buyers are responsive. It is inelastic when |E| < 1: quantity changes proportionally less than price, so buyers are unresponsive. It is unit elastic at the boundary |E| = 1. Visually, an inelastic demand curve is steep — a large price move buys only a small quantity response — while an elastic curve is flat.

Figure 5.1 — Inelastic (steep) versus elastic (flat) demand
Elastic, inelastic, unit elastic

Demand is elastic if |E| > 1 (quantity responds more than price), inelastic if |E| < 1 (quantity responds less than price), and unit elastic if |E| = 1 (equal proportional responses).

Two extremes bound the range. A perfectly inelastic demand (|E| = 0) is a vertical line: buyers take the same quantity no matter the price. A perfectly elastic demand (|E| = ∞) is a horizontal line: buyers will take any amount at one price and none above it.

Figure 5.2 — The two extremes
In the real world

Salt sits near the perfectly inelastic end: it has no real substitute, takes a trivial share of any budget, and a doubling of its price would barely dent how much people buy. A diabetic's insulin is similar — demand changes little with price, which is precisely why its pricing raises such hard questions. At the other end, the demand for one particular brand of cola is highly elastic: a small price rise sends buyers straight to the near-identical rival on the next shelf.

5.3  What determines elasticity

Why is demand for some goods elastic and others not? Four factors do most of the work. The first is the availability of close substitutes: the more easily buyers can switch to something else, the more elastic demand becomes — which is why a single brand is far more elastic than the whole category. The second is whether the good is a necessity or a luxury: necessities (basic food, medicine) hold demand steady through price changes, while luxuries (fine dining, second homes) are readily postponed. The third is the good's share of the budget: a price change in something trivial, like salt, is easy to ignore, whereas a change in a major expense, like rent, forces a response. The fourth is the time horizon.

In the real world

Gasoline is the classic illustration of the time horizon. In the short run its demand is quite inelastic: you own the car you own and must reach the job you have, so a price spike changes your driving only a little. Over years, however, demand becomes much more elastic — people buy more efficient cars, move closer to work, or shift to transit. The same good is inelastic this month and elastic this decade, which is why gasoline taxes change behavior far more in the long run than in the short.

5.4  Elasticity and total revenue

You discovered the central payoff at the start of the chapter: elasticity decides whether a price change raises or lowers total revenue (price times quantity). When demand is elastic, the quantity response outweighs the price change, so a price cut raises revenue and a price increase lowers it. When demand is inelastic, the reverse holds: a price increase raises revenue. At unit elasticity, revenue is at its peak and a small price move in either direction leaves it unchanged. This is the total-revenue test.

Figure 5.3 — Total revenue peaks where demand is unit elastic

A subtle but important point: elasticity is not the same as slope, and it is not constant along a straight-line demand curve. Moving down a linear demand curve, the same good is elastic near the top (high price, few buyers) and inelastic near the bottom (low price, many buyers), passing through unit elasticity exactly halfway — the point at which total revenue is greatest.

Figure 5.4 — Elasticity changes along a straight demand curve
⚠ Common pitfall — "the curve is straight, so the elasticity is constant"

A straight demand curve has a constant slope, but its elasticity changes at every point. Slope compares absolute changes (dollars and units); elasticity compares percentage changes, and the same one-dollar move is a large percentage at a low price and a small one at a high price. Treating a constant slope as a constant elasticity is one of the most common errors in the whole subject.

A shortcut: elasticity at a point on a linear demand curve

The midpoint formula gives the elasticity between two points. To find it at a single point — the natural question along a straight demand curve — there is a faster route, the point-elasticity formula:

\[ |E| = \left|\dfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P}\right| \times \dfrac{P}{Q} \]

The first term, ΔQ/ΔP, is the rate at which quantity changes as price changes — the inverse of the demand line's steepness — and for a straight line it is simply the constant slope coefficient. So read off any point (P, Q), multiply that coefficient by P/Q, and you are finished. This also explains why elasticity falls as you move down the curve: the slope term is fixed, but P/Q is large near the top (high price, few buyers) and small near the bottom, so |E| shrinks as the price falls.

Point elasticity

The price elasticity of demand at a single point on the curve, equal to (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q). On a straight-line demand curve the slope term is constant, so the elasticity is governed entirely by the ratio P/Q at the chosen point.

5.5  Three more elasticities worth knowing

The same idea — one percentage change divided by another — extends to three further measures, each with its own formula, sign convention, and use.

Price elasticity of supply

The price elasticity of supply measures how responsive quantity supplied is to a change in price:

\[ E_s = \dfrac{\%\,\Delta Q_s}{\%\,\Delta P} \]

It is positive, since higher prices draw out more output. A workshop with idle machines and available labor can expand quickly, so its supply is elastic; a vineyard, a semiconductor plant, or land in a prime location cannot, so their short-run supply is inelastic. Example: if a 10% rise in price brings a 25% rise in quantity supplied, then Es = 2.5 — elastic supply.

Income elasticity of demand

The income elasticity of demand measures how demand responds to a change in income (denoted I):

\[ E_I = \dfrac{\%\,\Delta Q_d}{\%\,\Delta I} \]

Its sign and size classify the good. It is positive for a normal good, greater than one for a luxury (demand grows faster than income), between zero and one for a necessity (demand grows more slowly than income), and negative for an inferior good (demand falls as income rises). Example: if a 10% rise in income raises restaurant spending 18%, then EI = 1.8 — a luxury; if it lowers bus ridership 4%, then EI = −0.4 — an inferior good.

Cross-price elasticity of demand

The cross-price elasticity of demand measures how the demand for one good (X) responds to a change in the price of another (Y):

\[ E_{xy} = \dfrac{\%\,\Delta Q_x}{\%\,\Delta P_y} \]

Here the sign carries the meaning. A positive value means the goods are substitutes — a rise in Y's price pushes buyers toward X; a negative value means they are complements — a rise in Y's price reduces demand for both. Example: if a 10% rise in the price of tea raises coffee demand 6%, then Exy = +0.6 (substitutes); if a 10% rise in the price of cars lowers gasoline demand 3%, then Exy = −0.3 (complements).

✦ Sharma's Take — the most underrated number in economics

Students tend to learn elasticity as a formula and move on. That is a mistake, because elasticity quietly determines the answer to nearly every applied question in economic policy. Who bears the burden of a tax? Mostly whichever side of the market is more inelastic — the side that cannot easily walk away. Why do farm prices collapse after a bumper harvest? Because demand for food is inelastic, so even a modest rise in quantity requires a steep fall in price to clear the market. The questions look unrelated until you see that each turns on an elasticity.

Poorly designed policy very often traces back to a misjudged elasticity. When one country imposed a steep luxury tax on yachts, it raised almost no revenue and gutted the domestic boatbuilding industry — because demand for luxury yachts is highly elastic, buyers simply postponed, bought abroad, or bought something else. So whenever you hear a confident claim about who will gain or lose from a policy, train yourself to ask one question first: what are the relevant elasticities? The honest answer often rewrites the entire story.

— A.S.

Government in the Market

When the state sets a price, taxes a sale, or subsidizes a purchase — and how supply, demand, and elasticity decide who actually gains, who pays, and what it costs in efficiency

The standard introductory treatment of government and markets tends to be Manichean: markets work, interventions distort, and the only honest analysis is to tally the loss and call for less of it. That is not economics so much as a political position dressed in economic language. The actual picture is more interesting. Markets sometimes work well and sometimes fail; interventions sometimes improve outcomes and sometimes worsen them. The serious question is never whether government should act, but when and how a given intervention helps or harms — and that question is answered with the supply-and-demand tools you already hold, sharpened by the elasticity you met in Chapter 5. This chapter studies the four most common interventions: price ceilings, price floors, taxes, and subsidies.

Start by stepping into the policymaker's chair.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

You set the price. What happens to the market?

A market clears on its own at $6, where 40 units trade. Suppose you legislate a different price instead. Slide it below the market price and then above it, and watch the quantity buyers want, the quantity sellers offer, and the gap that opens between them.

Below the market price, buyers want more than sellers will provide — a shortage. Above it, sellers offer more than buyers will take — a surplus. Either way, the quantity that actually trades is the smaller of the two: the market is only ever as large as its short side. Notice too that a price between the two does nothing at all.

6.1  Price ceilings: rent control

A price ceiling is a legal maximum price for a good. If it is set above the market price it does nothing — the market was already clearing below it, so the ceiling is non-binding. If it is set below the market price it binds, and at that lower price quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied: a shortage appears.

Price ceiling

A legal maximum price. A ceiling above the equilibrium price is non-binding and has no effect; a ceiling below the equilibrium price binds and produces a persistent shortage, because quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied.

The textbook case is rent control — a legal maximum on the rent a landlord may charge. The aim is to keep housing affordable for current tenants, and for them it often succeeds. But the diagram tells a fuller story.

Figure 6.1 — A binding rent ceiling produces a housing shortage

At the capped rent, far more renters want apartments than landlords are willing to supply. The shortage is only the beginning. Because the price can no longer ration housing, other margins take over, all of them documented consequences of rent control: landlords let buildings deteriorate, since they cannot recoup maintenance through higher rents; they convert rentals to condominiums or commercial use; new construction of rental housing slows, shrinking future supply; and the existing stock is misallocated, as tenants cling to below-market units they would otherwise leave. The apartment that does change hands is increasingly rationed by waiting lists, connections, and under-the-table payments rather than by price.

In the real world

Diamond, McQuade, and Qian (2019) studied an expansion of San Francisco's rent control using a difference-in-differences design. They found that landlords subject to control reduced rental supply in affected buildings by roughly 15% — converting and redeveloping to escape the rules — while protected tenants stayed about 19% longer. That supply contraction pushed citywide rents up by around 5%, so the policy delivered a large transfer to covered tenants while making housing less affordable for everyone else. Autor, Palmer, and Pathak (2014) found the mirror image when Cambridge, Massachusetts ended its rent control: property values rose sharply, implying the policy had been suppressing housing values all along. The consensus in housing economics is that rent control reliably helps current tenants in covered units yet worsens overall affordability — one of the clearest cases where the simple model's predictions hold up.

⚠ Common pitfall — "a ceiling makes the good cheaper"

A binding ceiling lowers the posted price, but only for those who manage to buy at it. For everyone shut out by the shortage, the effective price rises — paid in time spent searching, quality forgone, and side payments. The true cost of a rationed good includes all of these, which is why a ceiling can leave buyers as a group worse off even as the sticker price falls.

6.2  Price floors: the minimum wage

A price floor is a legal minimum price. Set below the market price it is non-binding; set above it, it binds, and quantity supplied then exceeds quantity demanded: a surplus appears. The minimum wage is a price floor on labor, and the simple model's prediction is immediate — a wage above the market-clearing level produces a surplus of labor, which is to say unemployment.

Price floor

A legal minimum price. A floor below the equilibrium price is non-binding; a floor above it binds and produces a surplus, because quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. In a labor market, that surplus is unemployment.

Figure 6.2 — A binding minimum wage produces a labor surplus

At the mandated wage, more people want to work than employers wish to hire; the gap between them is unemployment. That is the simple model. The empirical reality, however, turned out to be more contested than the diagram suggests — and the gap between the two is one of the most instructive episodes in modern economics.

In the real world

In a celebrated 1994 study, Card and Krueger compared fast-food employment in New Jersey, which had just raised its minimum wage, with neighboring Pennsylvania, which had not. The simple model predicted job losses in New Jersey; they found essentially none, and possibly a slight gain. That result launched three decades of careful work. The modern consensus, after Cengiz, Dube, Lindner, and Zipperer (2019) and many others, is that modest minimum-wage increases produce small employment effects, often statistically indistinguishable from zero, alongside clear and substantial wage gains for low-paid workers — though very large increases can begin to cost jobs. Why does the simple model overpredict the damage? Real labor markets have monopsony power (a few large employers with sway over wages), search frictions, and matching dynamics that absorb modest wage shocks. Seattle's move toward a $15 minimum produced a long, careful debate, but no employment collapse and real gains for low-wage workers. The lesson is not that the floor has no cost, but that the cost depends on how far above the market wage it is set — exactly the binding-versus-non-binding logic, with the labor market's real frictions layered on top.

6.3  Taxes and who really pays

A tax on a good places a wedge between the price the buyer pays and the price the seller keeps, the two differing by the tax. The wedge pushes the traded quantity below its untaxed level and raises revenue for the government. The central question is one of incidence: who actually bears the burden? And the surprising answer is that it does not depend on who writes the check.

Statutory versus economic incidence

Statutory incidence is who is legally required to remit the tax; economic incidence is who actually bears its burden through changed prices. The two need not coincide: a tax collected from sellers can be borne mostly by buyers, and vice versa.

What determines economic incidence is the relative elasticity of the two sides — the very tool from Chapter 5. The side of the market that is less elastic, less able to walk away, bears the larger share. Formally, in absolute-value elasticities:

\[ \text{buyers' share} = \dfrac{E_s}{E_s + E_d}, \qquad \text{sellers' share} = \dfrac{E_d}{E_s + E_d} \]

When demand is more inelastic than supply (small Ed), the buyers' share is large — buyers cannot easily flee the tax, so they absorb most of it. The two figures below show the same $3 tax in two markets that differ only in the elasticity of demand.

Worked example — reading incidence off the wedge
In Figure 6.3 the market originally clears at $10. After a $3 tax, buyers pay $12 and sellers keep $9. Who bears more, and by how much?
1
Buyers' burden. The buyer's price rose from $10 to $12 — an increase of $2.
2
Sellers' burden. The seller's price fell from $10 to $9 — a decrease of $1. The two burdens sum to the $3 tax, as they must.
3
Incidence. Buyers bear $2 of the $3, or two-thirds. This matches the formula: demand here is the more inelastic side, so buyers carry the larger share.

In the real world

Cigarettes are the textbook case of inelastic demand: addictive, with few substitutes and a modest budget share. So a cigarette tax falls overwhelmingly on smokers, who keep buying despite the higher price — which is exactly why such taxes raise dependable revenue while reducing quantity only modestly. It also illustrates a broader principle of tax design: a tax on an inelastic good produces less of the lost-trade waste economists call deadweight loss — the value of mutually beneficial trades that the wedge prevents — because few trades are deterred. Taxes on goods with genuine negative externalities (carbon, congestion, tobacco's harm to health) can do better still, nudging output back toward what is socially best rather than away from it.

⚠ Common pitfall — "whoever the law taxes is who pays"

Requiring sellers to remit a tax does not mean sellers bear it. They pass as much forward into the buyer's price as the elasticities allow. Conflating the legal payer (statutory incidence) with the real bearer (economic incidence) is among the most common errors in public debate — a payroll tax "split" between employer and worker, for instance, is borne largely by workers regardless of which half the statute assigns to whom.

6.4  Subsidies: when the government wants more

A subsidy is a government payment that lowers the effective price of a good — the mirror image of a tax. Where a tax opens a wedge that shrinks quantity, a subsidy opens one that expands it: the buyer pays less than the seller receives, with the government covering the difference, and more units trade than the free market would deliver.

Subsidy

A government payment that reduces the effective price of a good. It raises the quantity traded and opens a gap between the (lower) price buyers pay and the (higher) price sellers receive — the opposite of a tax wedge.

Figure 6.5 — A subsidy lowers the buyer's price, raises the seller's, and expands quantity

As with a tax, the incidence of a subsidy follows the elasticities: the more inelastic side captures more of the benefit. This has sharp policy consequences. A subsidy for first-time homebuyers in a city where housing supply is highly inelastic — most American cities — mostly raises prices, handing the benefit to home sellers rather than the buyers it was meant to help. A subsidy for solar-panel installation is split between homeowners (lower out-of-pocket cost) and installers and manufacturers (higher prices). Knowing which side is inelastic tells you, in advance, where the money will actually land.

In the real world

Subsidies are not merely taxes with a friendly face. For ordinary goods with no spillovers, a subsidy causes inefficient over-production, its own form of deadweight loss. But for goods with genuine positive externalities — benefits that spill onto others — a subsidy can raise total welfare, because the free market under-produces them. Vaccination protects more than the vaccinated; basic research benefits everyone; education raises the capability of the whole workforce; clean energy reduces shared environmental harm. In each case the unsubsidized market makes too little, and a well-aimed subsidy moves output toward the social optimum. The 2022 CHIPS Act (semiconductors) and Inflation Reduction Act (clean energy) are large recent bets on exactly this logic. The trick, always, is design: targeting activities with real externalities, sizing the subsidy correctly, and not simply paying interests who would have acted anyway.

✦ Sharma's Take — the question is when and how, not whether

It is tempting to collapse this whole chapter into a slogan — intervention bad, market good — but every case here resists it. Rent control genuinely helps the tenants it covers and genuinely harms future renters; the minimum wage lifts low wages with employment costs that depend on how high it is set; taxes fund public goods while deterring some trade; subsidies waste resources on ordinary goods yet correct real failures for goods with externalities. None of these is simply good or bad. Each is a transfer with an efficiency cost, and the economist's job is to trace, honestly, who gains, who pays, and whether the gain is worth the cost.

That tracing is where elasticity earns its keep. Whoever is inelastic — the renter who cannot move, the smoker who cannot quit, the landowner who cannot make more land — bears the burden of a tax and captures the benefit of a subsidy, no matter what the statute says or the politics claims. And the politics often runs the other way from the economics: rent control persists because its winners are visible and organized while its losers are diffuse and not yet in the room. Seeing past that, to who actually bears the weight, is the entire value of the framework. Keep asking the one question that cuts through: what are the relevant elasticities, and where does the burden truly fall?

— A.S.

Chapter 7 · Part II: How Markets Work

Welfare: Surplus & Efficiency

How to measure what a market is worth to the people in it — and how to put an exact number on the cost of pushing it away from where it wants to be

Twice already this book has leaned on words it had not yet earned. In Chapter 4 the market equilibrium was called a good outcome; in Chapter 6 every intervention was charged with a "deadweight loss." Both claims assumed we could measure how much a market is worth to its participants. This chapter builds that measure. It turns out that the demand and supply curves you already know contain, hidden in plain sight, the value buyers place on a good and the cost sellers bear to make it — and from those two we can compute precisely what a market gains by operating freely, and exactly what it loses when it does not.

Begin with a decision that exposes the whole idea.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

How many units should trade?

The demand curve gives the value buyers place on each successive unit; the supply curve gives the cost of producing it. You decide how many units change hands. Slide the quantity and compare the value of the last unit to its cost — then find the quantity that wrings the most total benefit out of the market.

As long as the next unit is worth more than it costs, trading it adds value. Once the next unit costs more than it is worth, trading it subtracts value. The best quantity is exactly where value meets cost — which is the competitive equilibrium. The market, left alone, lands there on its own.

7.1  Willingness to pay and consumer surplus

Every buyer has a willingness to pay — the most they would give up for a unit of a good rather than go without. The demand curve is simply every buyer's willingness to pay, lined up from highest to lowest: its height at each quantity is the value the marginal buyer places on that unit. When the market price is below a buyer's willingness to pay, the buyer pockets the difference. That gain, summed over all buyers, is consumer surplus.

Willingness to pay & consumer surplus

Willingness to pay is the maximum a buyer would pay for a good. Consumer surplus is the difference between total willingness to pay and the amount actually paid — on the diagram, the area below the demand curve and above the price, up to the quantity bought.

Figure 7.1 — Consumer surplus is the area below demand and above the price

In our market the price is $6 and 40 units are bought. The highest-value buyer would have paid nearly $10 but pays only $6, and every buyer down to the fortieth captures some surplus. Because the region is a triangle, its area is straightforward:

\( \text{CS} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 40 \times (10 - 6) = 80 \)

In the real world

You see consumer surplus whenever you would have paid more than you were charged. If you would have paid $50 for a concert ticket and bought it for $30, you walk away with $20 of consumer surplus. A single posted price serves a crowd of buyers with very different valuations, and almost all of them come out ahead of what they paid — which is why a working market quietly generates enormous value that never appears on any receipt.

7.2  Cost and producer surplus

Sellers have the mirror-image quantity. Each unit has a marginal cost — the least a seller would accept to produce it, covering the resources it consumes. The supply curve lines these costs up from lowest to highest: its height at each quantity is the cost of the marginal unit. When the price exceeds that cost, the seller keeps the difference, and the sum over all units is producer surplus.

Marginal cost & producer surplus

Marginal cost is the least a seller would accept for a unit (the cost of producing it). Producer surplus is the difference between the price received and that minimum, summed over all units — on the diagram, the area above the supply curve and below the price.

Figure 7.2 — Producer surplus is the area above supply and below the price

The lowest-cost unit costs only $2 to make but sells for $6; the fortieth costs exactly $6, earning no surplus. The triangle gives:

\( \text{PS} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 40 \times (6 - 2) = 80 \)

7.3  Total surplus and the efficiency of equilibrium

Add the two together and you have total surplus — the full gains from trade the market creates, shared between buyers and sellers. Here it is $80 + $80 = $160. The diagram stacks the two regions: the whole wedge between the demand and supply curves, from the first unit to the last.

Figure 7.3 — Total surplus = consumer surplus + producer surplus
Total surplus & efficiency

Total surplus is consumer surplus plus producer surplus — the total value a market creates. An outcome is efficient when total surplus is at its maximum, so that no rearrangement of production or trade could make someone better off without making someone else worse off.

Here is the central result, the precise version of the "invisible hand": the competitive equilibrium maximizes total surplus. The reasoning is the marginal logic you used in the interactive. At any quantity below equilibrium, the next unit is worth more to a buyer than it costs a seller, so trading it would enlarge the pie — surplus is being left unrealized. At any quantity above equilibrium, the next unit costs more than it is worth, so trading it shrinks the pie. Only at the equilibrium quantity, where value exactly equals cost, is every worthwhile trade made and no wasteful one — and total surplus reaches its peak. No planner directs this; the price alone coordinates it.

7.4  Deadweight loss: the cost of getting it wrong

Now the promise from Chapter 6 can be paid off. Anything that pushes the traded quantity away from the efficient level — a tax, a price ceiling, a price floor, a monopoly — prevents some mutually beneficial trades. The surplus those lost trades would have generated is gone, captured by no one. That pure, vanished value is the deadweight loss.

Deadweight loss

The reduction in total surplus caused by moving the quantity traded away from the efficient level. It measures the value of mutually beneficial trades that no longer occur — a loss to society that becomes no one's gain.

Figure 7.4 — A tax shrinks quantity; the lost surplus is the deadweight-loss triangle

A $4 tax opens a wedge between the $8 buyers pay and the $4 sellers keep, and quantity falls from 40 to 20. On the 20 units no longer traded, buyers valued each above what sellers would have charged — those gains simply disappear. The lost surplus is the triangle between the demand and supply curves over the missing units:

\( \text{DWL} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 20 \times 4 = 40 \)

The same triangle appears, in its own shape, behind every intervention from Chapter 6: the rent ceiling that leaves apartments unbuilt, the minimum wage that leaves willing workers unhired, the subsidy that funds units worth less than they cost. In each case the deadweight loss measures the trades that should have happened and did not, or that happened and should not have.

⚠ Common pitfall — "the tax revenue is the cost of the tax"

A tax's revenue is not a loss to society; it is a transfer — surplus moved from buyers and sellers to the government, which spends it on something. The genuine economic cost of the tax is only the deadweight loss: the surplus that no one captures because the trades vanished. This is why a small tax on an inelastic good can raise real revenue at little true cost, while a large tax on an elastic good can destroy far more surplus than it ever collects.

✦ Sharma's Take — efficiency is a powerful lens, not the whole picture

Total surplus is one of the most useful constructs in economics, and also one of the most quietly opinionated. Notice what it does: it adds up everyone's gains in dollars and treats each dollar the same, whether it lands with a billionaire or someone choosing between rent and food. That is a real assumption, not a neutral fact — a normative choice wearing the clothes of a positive measurement. Maximizing total surplus tells you how to make the pie as large as possible. It says nothing about whether the slices are fairly cut.

This is why "the efficient outcome" and "the right outcome" are not synonyms. A policy can raise total surplus while concentrating the gains among those who already have the most; another can lower total surplus slightly while sharply improving the lot of the worst off. Reasonable people weigh efficiency against equity differently, and that weighing is a question of values, not of diagrams. What the framework offers is discipline: it lets you measure the size of the efficiency cost precisely, so that when you choose to accept one for the sake of fairness, you know exactly what you are trading and can defend the trade honestly. Use the tool for what it measures — and stay honest about what it leaves out.

Chapter 8 · Part III: The Consumer & the Firm

Consumer Choice

Where the demand curve actually comes from — how a buyer with limited income and unlimited wants squeezes the most satisfaction from each dollar, and why that simple rule bends demand downward

The demand curve has been a given since Chapter 4: it slopes down, it shifts, it carries willingness to pay. But where does it come from? Behind every market demand curve stands a crowd of individual buyers, each facing the same basic problem — limited income, competing wants, and a decision about how to spread money across goods. This chapter solves that problem, and in doing so rebuilds the demand curve from the ground up. The tool is the marginal thinking of Chapter 1, now pointed at the shopping cart.

Start, as always, by deciding.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

Spend $18 to get the most satisfaction

Mara has $18. Coffee costs $2 a cup, pastries $3 each, and each gives her "utility" — satisfaction — with later units worth less than earlier ones. Slide how many coffees she buys (the rest of the budget goes to pastries) and compare the satisfaction-per-dollar of her last coffee against her last pastry. Find the split that wrings out the most total utility.

When one good delivers more utility per dollar than the other, you can do better by shifting a dollar toward it. You stop improving only when the two bars are level — when the last dollar spent on each buys the same amount of satisfaction. That balance is the consumer's optimum.

8.1  The budget constraint

Choice begins with what is affordable. The budget constraint is the set of all combinations of goods a consumer can buy with a given income at given prices. With income I and two goods priced Px and Py, the combinations that spend the budget exactly satisfy:

\( P_x \, x + P_y \, y = I \)

Plotted, this is the budget line, and its intercepts are simply how much of each good the whole income would buy. Its slope is the ratio of prices, −Px/Py — the rate at which the market lets the consumer trade one good for the other. A higher income pushes the whole line outward without changing its slope; a change in one price pivots it.

Budget constraint & budget line

The budget constraint is every bundle a consumer can afford at given income and prices. The budget line is its boundary; its slope, −Px/Py, is the market rate at which one good can be traded for the other.

Figure 8.1 — A rise in income shifts the budget line outward

A change in income moves the line in parallel, because the price ratio — the slope — is unchanged. A change in a single price is different: it swings the line around the unaffected intercept, because the relative price has changed. When coffee gets cheaper, the same $18 reaches more coffee but the same pastries, so the line pivots outward along the coffee axis alone.

Figure 8.2 — A fall in one price pivots the budget line

8.2  Utility and diminishing marginal utility

To choose among affordable bundles, the consumer needs a way to rank them. Economists use utility — a measure of satisfaction. Total utility is the satisfaction from all units consumed; marginal utility is the additional satisfaction from one more unit. The crucial empirical regularity is diminishing marginal utility: each successive unit of a good adds less satisfaction than the one before. The first coffee of the morning is a delight; the fourth, merely pleasant; the seventh, barely wanted.

Utility, marginal utility, diminishing marginal utility

Total utility is the satisfaction from all units consumed; marginal utility is the extra satisfaction from one more unit. Diminishing marginal utility is the tendency for marginal utility to fall as more of a good is consumed.

Figure 8.3 — Marginal utility falls as consumption rises

8.3  The consumer's optimum: the equimarginal rule

Now combine the two halves. The consumer wants the most total utility the budget allows. The key insight is that what matters is not utility alone but utility per dollar — how much satisfaction each dollar buys. A dollar spent on coffee buys MUcoffee/Pcoffee of utility; a dollar on pastries buys MUpastry/Ppastry. If these differ, the consumer can gain by moving a dollar from the lower to the higher. Only when they are equal is no further gain possible. That is the equimarginal rule:

\[ \dfrac{MU_x}{P_x} = \dfrac{MU_y}{P_y} \]

The equimarginal rule

A consumer maximizes utility by allocating the budget so that the marginal utility per dollar is equal across all goods. If one good offers more utility per dollar, shifting spending toward it raises total utility — until equality is restored.

Worked example — finding the optimal bundle
With $18, coffee at $2 and pastries at $3, and the diminishing utilities in the interactive, which bundle is best?
1
Spend on the highest utility-per-dollar first. Buy each next unit wherever the marginal utility per dollar is greatest, working down as diminishing utility lowers each good's appeal.
2
Stop when the budget is exhausted. The $18 runs out at 3 coffees and 4 pastries ($6 + $12 = $18).
3
Check the rule. The third coffee yields 12 utils at $2 → 6 per dollar; the fourth pastry yields 18 utils at $3 → 6 per dollar. Equal, as the rule requires. Total utility is 141 — the most $18 can buy.

8.4  Indifference curves and the optimum bundle

The equimarginal rule found the optimum by measuring utility in units. That is convenient but demanding — real consumers cannot report "twelve utils." A second approach reaches the same optimum while assuming far less: it asks only that a consumer be able to say which of two bundles they prefer, or whether they are indifferent between them. Its central tool is the indifference curve.

An indifference curve connects all the bundles that give a consumer equal satisfaction — bundles among which they are, by definition, indifferent. A whole map of them describes the consumer's preferences: curves farther from the origin contain more of both goods and so are preferred, and two curves can never cross. Each curve slopes downward (giving up one good requires more of the other to stay equally satisfied) and bows inward toward the origin.

Figure 8.5 — An indifference map: higher curves are preferred
Indifference curve

A curve showing all bundles of goods that give a consumer equal satisfaction. Higher curves (farther from the origin) are preferred; the curves slope downward, never cross, and bow toward the origin.

The slope of an indifference curve has a name: the marginal rate of substitution (MRS), the amount of one good a consumer will give up for one more unit of the other while remaining equally satisfied. It equals the ratio of the goods' marginal utilities, and it diminishes as you move down the curve — the more coffee you already hold, the fewer pastries you will sacrifice for one more cup. That diminishing willingness to trade is exactly what makes the curve bow inward.

\[ MRS = \dfrac{MU_x}{MU_y} = \dfrac{P_x}{P_y} \]

Marginal rate of substitution

The rate at which a consumer will trade one good for another while staying equally satisfied — the slope of the indifference curve, equal to the ratio of marginal utilities MUx/MUy. It diminishes as one moves along the curve.

Now overlay the budget line. The consumer wants the highest indifference curve the budget can reach. A curve that crosses the budget line is never the best: crossing means part of that curve lies beyond the line, where still-higher affordable bundles sit. The best the budget allows is the curve that merely touches the line — the point of tangency. There the two slopes are equal, which gives the optimum condition: the marginal rate of substitution equals the price ratio, \( MRS = P_x/P_y \). Because MRS = MUx/MUy, this is the very same condition as the equimarginal rule — rearrange MUx/MUy = Px/Py and you recover MUx/Px = MUy/Py. Two routes, one optimum.

Figure 8.6 — The optimum is where the budget line is tangent to the highest reachable curve

For our running example — $18, coffee at $2, pastries at $3 — the tangency falls at 3 coffees and 4 pastries, precisely the bundle the equimarginal rule selected. Hunt for it yourself.

⚙ Explore — reach the highest curve

Slide along the budget line and find the tangency

Every point on the line spends the whole $18. As you move, the indifference curve through your bundle is drawn. When that curve cuts across the line, you can still do better; when it only just touches, you have found the best bundle the budget allows.

8.5  From the optimum to the demand curve

This machinery answers the chapter's opening question. Suppose the price of coffee falls. Two forces push the consumer to buy more of it. The substitution effect: coffee is now cheaper relative to pastries, so the consumer swaps toward it. The income effect: the lower price stretches the same income further, raising purchasing power as though income had risen, and a normal good is bought more when purchasing power grows. Both effects point the same way — a lower price, a larger quantity demanded — which is the law of demand, now derived rather than assumed.

Substitution and income effects

When a price falls, the substitution effect leads the consumer to buy more of the now-relatively-cheaper good; the income effect leads them to buy more because their purchasing power has effectively risen. Together they explain why demand slopes downward.

The indifference-curve apparatus makes the derivation exact. Hold income and the price of pastries fixed and let coffee's price fall step by step. Each lower price pivots the budget line outward along the coffee axis, and each pivoted line has its own tangency — its own optimal bundle. The trail of those optimal bundles is the price-consumption path.

Figure 8.7 — As coffee's price falls, the budget pivots and the optimal bundle moves right

Read the price and the optimal coffee quantity off each tangency — at $3 the consumer buys 2 cups, at $2 buys 3, at $1 buys 6 — and plot price against quantity. The result is the coffee demand curve, now constructed directly from the consumer's own preferences and budget rather than assumed.

Figure 8.8 — The coffee demand curve, read off the price-consumption path

8.6  The diamond-water paradox

The distinction between total and marginal utility resolves a puzzle that troubled economists for centuries. Water is essential to life; diamonds are mere ornament. Why, then, do diamonds command a vastly higher price? Because price reflects marginal utility, not total. Water's total utility is immense, but it is so abundant that the value of one more glass — its marginal utility — is nearly nothing, so its price is low. Diamonds are scarce, so the value of one more is high, and so is the price. The enormous gap between water's total value and its trivial price is precisely the consumer surplus of Chapter 7 — a benefit we enjoy without paying for it.

✦ Sharma's Take — the most portable rule in economics

Take the equimarginal rule seriously and it escapes the shopping cart entirely. Its real content is this: to allocate any scarce resource well, spread it so that the last unit devoted to each use yields the same marginal return. That governs far more than money. How should you split a fixed study week across five courses? Put the next hour where it raises your grade most, until the marginal hour is equally valuable everywhere. How should a firm divide a budget across projects, a government across programs, a person across the competing claims on their attention? The same logic. Equalize at the margin. Once you see it, you see it everywhere.

A caution keeps the model honest. No one literally computes marginal utility per dollar at the register, and "utility" is not happiness measured in real units — it is a modeling device, an as-if. People use rules of thumb, miscount, and are nudged by how choices are framed; the later chapters on behavioral economics take this seriously. Yet for all that, the model earns its place: on average, across many buyers and many choices, people do shift toward what gives them more for less, and demand curves do slope down. The equimarginal rule is not a literal description of thought. It is a remarkably good description of behavior — which, for a science of choice, is what counts.

Production & Costs

Where the supply curve is born — how a firm turns inputs into output, why costs rise the way they do, and the single relationship between productivity and cost that shapes every supply decision

The last chapter built the demand curve from the buyer's decision. This one crosses the market to the seller and does the mirror-image work. Behind every supply curve stands a firm transforming inputs — labor, materials, equipment — into output, and paying for the privilege. To understand supply we must understand production and the costs it generates. By the chapter's end the cost curves will be in hand, and the very next chapter will turn them into the supply curve itself.

Begin where the firm's manager begins: with a hiring decision.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

How many workers should the cart hire?

Mara's coffee cart is fixed in size. You add baristas one at a time and watch daily output climb. Pay attention to how much each new worker adds — not the total, but the extra. Somewhere along the way, that extra starts to shrink.

The first few workers raise output sharply — they can specialize and share the equipment well. But with the cart fixed, each later worker has less room and less machine to work with, so the extra output shrinks. That shrinking extra is the engine of rising costs, and the heart of this chapter.

9.1  The production function and the short run

A firm's production function describes the most output it can produce from each combination of inputs. Inputs come in two kinds, and the distinction defines the time horizon. A fixed input cannot be changed quickly — the cart, the factory, the lease; a variable input can — labor, raw materials. The short run is the period in which at least one input is fixed; the long run is long enough that all inputs can vary. These are not fixed clock-times: for a food cart the short run might be weeks, for a power plant, years.

Production function, short run, long run

The production function gives the maximum output obtainable from given inputs. The short run is the period in which at least one input is fixed; the long run is the period in which all inputs are variable.

Holding the cart fixed and adding workers, total output — the total product — rises, quickly at first and then more slowly, eventually flattening as the fixed cart becomes crowded.

Figure 9.1 — Total product rises, then flattens as the fixed input is crowded

9.2  Marginal product and diminishing returns

The telling quantity is not the total but the increment. The marginal product of an input is the additional output from one more unit of it — one more worker. Early on, marginal product may even rise as workers specialize. But because the cart is fixed, a point arrives beyond which each additional worker has less capital and space to work with, and adds less than the last. This is the law of diminishing marginal returns: with at least one input fixed, the marginal product of a variable input eventually falls.

Marginal product & diminishing marginal returns

Marginal product is the extra output from one more unit of an input. The law of diminishing marginal returns states that, holding other inputs fixed, the marginal product of a variable input eventually declines as more of it is used.

Figure 9.2 — Marginal product rises, peaks, then diminishes

Diminishing returns is not a claim that workers are lazy or technology is poor. It is a statement about a fixed factor: pile more and more of a variable input onto an unchanging cart, field, or factory, and the fixed thing is spread ever thinner. It is the central fact of short-run production, and it drives everything about cost that follows.

9.3  From production to cost

Producing output means paying for inputs, so the production function has a cost twin. Costs split by whether they vary with output. Fixed cost (FC) does not change with output — the cart lease, due whether you sell zero cups or a hundred. Variable cost (VC) rises with output — wages, beans, cups. Their sum is total cost (TC = FC + VC). Dividing each by output gives the per-unit measures: average fixed cost (AFC = FC/Q), average variable cost (AVC = VC/Q), and average total cost (ATC = TC/Q), with:

\( ATC = AFC + AVC \)

The most important cost of all is the marginal cost (MC) — the extra cost of producing one more unit. And here production and cost lock together. If one more worker costs the wage w and produces MP extra units, then the extra cost per unit is simply:

\[ MC = \dfrac{\Delta TC}{\Delta Q} = \dfrac{w}{MP} \]

This is the hinge of the whole chapter. Marginal cost is the wage divided by marginal product, so the two move as mirror images: when marginal product is high, marginal cost is low; as diminishing returns drive marginal product down, marginal cost is driven up. Rising marginal cost is diminishing returns wearing a price tag.

The cost family

Fixed cost is independent of output; variable cost rises with it; total cost is their sum. Average costs are these divided by output; marginal cost is the extra cost of one more unit, equal to the wage divided by the marginal product.

Worked example — reading the cost curves
The firm's fixed cost is $48 a day. Producing 20 cups costs $72 in variable cost. Find average total cost at 20 cups, and the marginal cost there.
1
Total cost. TC = FC + VC = $48 + $72 = $120.
2
Average total cost. ATC = $120 / 20 = $6.00 per cup.
3
Marginal cost. At 20 cups the firm sits at the bottom of its U-shaped ATC curve — exactly where marginal cost equals average total cost — so MC here is also $6.00.

9.4  The shape of the cost curves

Plot the per-unit costs against output and a characteristic picture emerges. Average fixed cost falls steadily, as the fixed lease is spread over ever more cups. Marginal cost and average total cost are U-shaped: falling while early workers are highly productive, then rising as diminishing returns take hold. And the marginal-cost curve passes through the lowest points of both average curves — a necessary relationship, not a coincidence.

Figure 9.3 — Marginal cost cuts average total and average variable cost at their minimums

Why must marginal cost cross the averages at their lowest points? Because of simple arithmetic: whenever the cost of the next unit is below the average, it pulls the average down; whenever it is above, it pushes the average up. The average therefore stops falling and starts rising exactly where marginal cost equals it — at the bottom of the U. The same logic governs any average and its marginal: a student whose next exam beats their average raises it, and is overtaken precisely when the new score equals the old average.

⚠ Common pitfall — confusing average and marginal cost

Average total cost is the cost per unit so far; marginal cost is the cost of the next unit. They answer different questions and usually differ. When marginal cost is below average, the average is still falling even though the next unit is cheap relative to the average; when marginal is above, the average rises. Decisions about whether to make one more unit hinge on marginal cost, never on the average.

9.5  The long run: economies and diseconomies of scale

In the long run nothing is fixed — the firm can build a bigger cart, a second location, a whole factory. Now the question is not diminishing returns to a crowded fixed input but returns to scale: how cost per unit behaves as the entire operation grows. The long-run average cost curve is typically U-shaped for a different reason. At small scale there are economies of scale: cost per unit falls as size rises, through specialization, bulk buying, and spreading large fixed investments over more output. Eventually diseconomies of scale set in: the organization grows unwieldy, coordination and communication falter, and cost per unit climbs.

Economies & diseconomies of scale

Economies of scale exist where long-run average cost falls as output rises; diseconomies of scale where it rises. Between them may lie a range of roughly constant returns — the efficient scale of the industry.

Figure 9.4 — Long-run average cost: economies, then diseconomies of scale
In the real world

Economies of scale explain why some industries are dominated by giants and others by small firms. Electricity generation, semiconductor fabrication, and automobile assembly carry enormous fixed costs that only huge output can spread thin, so they tend toward a few large producers. Restaurants, hair salons, and consulting shops exhaust their economies of scale quickly — a second chef adds little a first did not — and coordination costs bite early, so these industries stay populated by many small firms. The shape of the long-run cost curve quietly shapes the structure of the entire industry, which is the subject of Part IV.

✦ Sharma's Take — cost is the hidden architecture of supply

Students often treat cost curves as a dry bookkeeping exercise, but they are the structural steel behind everything that follows. The marginal-cost curve, in particular, is about to become the firm's supply curve almost without alteration — a competitive firm produces where price meets marginal cost, so the upward-sloping MC curve is the supply of the firm. Every claim about supply you have used since Chapter 4 traces back to the diminishing returns and rising marginal cost built here. Master this picture and supply stops being an assumption and becomes a consequence.

Two cautions repay attention. First, diminishing returns is not a gloomy verdict on human productivity; it is a short-run statement about a fixed factor, and the long run — where the factory itself can grow — tells a different, scale-driven story. Confusing the two leads to bad forecasts. Second, fixed costs are a trap for decision-making. Because fixed cost does not change with output, it should play no part in the choice of whether to make one more unit, or even whether to stay open today — that choice turns on marginal cost and variable cost alone. The lease you have already committed to is the sunk cost of Chapter 1 in a business suit: real money, but irrelevant at the margin. The firms that thrive are the ones that feel its weight and still decide at the margin anyway.

Perfect Competition

The benchmark market — how a firm with no power over price chooses its output, and how, at last, the supply curve is derived rather than assumed

Part IV asks how the structure of a market — how many firms, how similar their products, how easily new ones enter — shapes its behavior. Economists distinguish four structures along a spectrum of market power. Perfect competition has many firms selling an identical product with free entry, so no firm has any power over price. Monopolistic competition has many firms selling differentiated products. Oligopoly has a few interdependent firms. Monopoly has a single seller with great power over price. We start at the powerless extreme, because it is both the simplest case and the benchmark against which every other is judged — and because it is here that the supply curve, assumed since Chapter 4, is finally built from the cost curves of Chapter 9.

Step into the firm's decision.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

The market sets a price. How much should the firm make?

Mara's cart is one of thousands selling identical coffee, so she cannot influence the market price — she can only react to it. Set the price the market hands her and watch her best response: the output where the next cup's price just covers its marginal cost, and whether she profits, loses, or should close for the day.

At every price, the firm produces where price meets marginal cost. Sweep the price upward and that meeting-point slides up the MC curve — tracing out the firm's supply curve. Drop the price below minimum average variable cost and the firm stops producing altogether. You are deriving supply.

10.1  The competitive firm is a price taker

Because a perfectly competitive firm is one tiny seller of an identical product among many, it has no power to set price: it is a price taker, able to sell as much as it likes at the going market price but nothing at all above it. This has a sharp consequence. The demand curve facing the firm is horizontal — perfectly elastic — at the market price, even though the demand curve facing the whole market slopes down. And since every unit sells for the same price, the revenue from one more unit — marginal revenue — is simply that price. For a competitive firm, price equals marginal revenue.

Price taker

A firm with no influence over the market price, which it must accept as given. It faces a horizontal (perfectly elastic) demand curve at that price, so for it price equals marginal revenue: P = MR.

Figure 10.1 — The firm faces a flat demand at the market price, though the market's slopes down

10.2  Profit maximization: produce where P = MC

How much should such a firm produce? Apply the marginal logic. Each additional unit adds its price to revenue and its marginal cost to cost. As long as the price exceeds marginal cost, the unit adds to profit and is worth making; once marginal cost rises above price, further units subtract. Profit is therefore greatest at the output where the two are equal. Since P = MR for a price taker, the universal profit-maximizing rule MR = MC becomes, in competition:

\[ P = MR = MC \]

Whether that profitable-looking output actually earns a profit depends on average total cost. Profit per unit is price minus average total cost, so:

\( \text{Profit} = (P - ATC) \times Q \)

The profit-maximizing rule

A competitive firm maximizes profit by producing the quantity where price equals marginal cost (P = MC). It earns a profit if price exceeds average total cost at that quantity, and a loss if price falls short.

10.3  The firm's short-run outcomes

The rule P = MC fixes how much the firm makes. Whether that output earns a profit, breaks even, or runs a loss depends on where the price sits relative to the firm's average curves — and all of it is read off a single cost diagram. Begin with the loss case, because it carries a surprise: a firm losing money may still be right to keep producing. The reason is fixed cost. It must be paid whether or not the firm operates, so it is irrelevant to the short-run decision; what matters is whether revenue covers variable cost. If price is at least average variable cost, operating earns something toward the unavoidable fixed cost. Only when price falls below minimum average variable cost does every unit deepen the loss — and then the firm should shut down, losing only its fixed cost.

Putting the cases together, the firm faces four possibilities as price rises past its cost benchmarks (here minimum AVC is $3 and minimum ATC is $6):

The four short-run outcomes

Profit when price exceeds average total cost (P > ATC): the firm covers every cost and keeps a surplus of (P − ATC) × Q. Break-even when price equals minimum average total cost (P = $6): economic profit is exactly zero. Loss, but operate when price lies between minimum AVC and minimum ATC ($3 ≤ P < $6): the firm loses money yet produces, because price covers variable cost and chips away at fixed cost. Shut down when price falls below minimum AVC (P < $3): the firm produces nothing and loses only its fixed cost.

10.4  Deriving the firm's supply curve

These outcomes pin down exactly how much the firm offers at every price — which is precisely what a supply curve records. At any price at or above minimum average variable cost, the firm produces where P = MC, so its chosen quantity is read straight off the marginal-cost curve. Below that price it offers nothing. Therefore the firm's short-run supply curve is its marginal-cost curve above minimum average variable cost — here, the part of MC at or above the $3 shutdown point. This is the firm-level supply relationship we assumed on faith back in Chapter 4, now derived from the firm's own costs.

The firm's short-run supply curve

A competitive firm's short-run supply curve is the portion of its marginal-cost curve lying at or above minimum average variable cost. At lower prices the firm supplies zero.

10.5  From the short run to the long run

In the short run this firm can profit or lose, because its plant — the cart, the counter space — is fixed. In the long run it can vary everything, and in a competitive industry firms are also free to enter or leave. That freedom has a sharp consequence for the individual firm's bottom line. If a firm like this one is earning profit, the opportunity draws entrants whose added output pushes the market price down; if it is taking losses, some firms exit and the thinning supply lifts the price back up. The drift stops only when price has settled at the firm's minimum average total cost — $6 here — where economic profit is exactly zero. At that price the firm produces at the very bottom of its ATC curve, q = 20, its efficient scale.

Economic versus accounting profit

Accounting profit is revenue minus explicit money costs. Economic profit also subtracts opportunity costs — what the owner's money, time, and effort could have earned elsewhere. Zero economic profit means the firm earns exactly a normal return: enough to stay, but no more than its resources could make elsewhere.

✦ Sharma's Take — a market that exists mostly as a yardstick

Perfect competition almost never holds in its pure form — truly identical products and truly powerless firms are rare. Students sometimes conclude the model is therefore useless. The opposite is true: its value is precisely as a benchmark. Two of its results make it the reference point for the whole field. First, in equilibrium price equals marginal cost, which from Chapter 7 is exactly the condition for allocative efficiency — the last unit is worth to buyers exactly what it costs society to make. Perfect competition is the case where the invisible hand works perfectly, and every other market structure is measured by how far it falls short of this. Second, free entry drives long-run economic profit to zero — not because firms earn nothing, but because they earn exactly a normal return once opportunity cost is counted.

That second point is the most misunderstood result in microeconomics. "Zero profit" sounds like failure; it is nothing of the kind. Economic profit subtracts what the owner could have earned elsewhere, so zero economic profit means the business is doing exactly as well as its next-best alternative — a perfectly healthy, sustainable state. When you hear that competition "competes away the profits," translate it correctly: competition competes away the excess, the returns above normal, and passes them to consumers as lower prices. That is not the model failing. That is the model describing what competition is for.

— A.S.

Monopoly

The opposite pole from perfect competition — a single seller that chooses price rather than taking it, why such firms arise, the cost their power imposes on society, and what a regulator can do about it

In Chapter 10 the firm was powerless: one of thousands selling an identical product, forced to accept whatever price the market handed it. This chapter visits the other extreme. A monopoly is a market with a single seller of a product that has no close substitutes. Because it is the market, the monopolist does not take the price as given — it chooses, sliding along the market demand curve to whatever price-quantity combination serves it best. That power is the whole story of this chapter: where it comes from, how the firm uses it, what it costs the rest of us, and how governments try to rein it in.

Monopoly

A market served by a single seller of a good with no close substitutes, protected by barriers that keep rivals out. The monopolist is a price maker: it faces the entire downward-sloping market demand curve and selects the point on it that maximizes profit.

Our running vendor, Mara, gives us the case. Suppose her cart holds the only coffee licence in a small mountain town in Verenia: no other seller is allowed to operate, and the nearest café is an hour's drive away. She is now a monopolist, and the town's entire demand for coffee is hers to face: \( P = 12 - 0.2Q \), price in dollars, \(Q\) in cups per day.

11.1  Why monopolies exist

A single seller can only persist if something keeps competitors out. Anything that does so is a barrier to entry, and essentially every real monopoly rests on one of four.

Barrier to entry

A factor that prevents or strongly discourages new firms from entering a market. The four classic sources are economies of scale, legal protection, control of a key resource, and network effects.

Economies of scale (the natural monopoly). When average total cost keeps falling as output rises over the entire range the market demands, a single large firm can serve everyone more cheaply than several smaller ones could. Any entrant starts small, with high average cost, and cannot undercut the incumbent. Water, electricity, and gas distribution are the textbook cases: the pipes and wires cost a fortune to lay and almost nothing to run, so one network beats two. This case is important enough that §11.4 returns to it in detail.

Legal barriers. Governments deliberately create monopolies when they judge the trade worthwhile. A patent grants an inventor roughly two decades of exclusive rights, so that the prospect of monopoly profit rewards — and thereby encourages — costly research; this is why a new drug can cost pennies to manufacture yet sell for hundreds of dollars until its patent expires. Licences and franchises hand a single firm the sole right to operate, as with Mara's town coffee licence, a regional utility, or a broadcast spectrum band.

Control of a key resource. A firm that owns the essential input can deny it to everyone else. The classic example is the De Beers diamond cartel, which for much of the twentieth century controlled the bulk of the world's rough-diamond supply and with it the price.

Network effects. Some products grow more valuable the more people use them, so an early lead can tip into dominance: a social network, a marketplace, or a payment system is useful precisely because everyone else is already on it, and a newcomer offering the same features to an empty room cannot compete. Much of the market power of the largest technology platforms rests here.

Common pitfall. A monopoly is not simply a very large or very profitable firm. What defines it is the absence of close substitutes and the barrier that keeps rivals out. A giant firm in a contestable market — one that new entrants could join tomorrow — behaves far more competitively than a small firm sheltered behind a legal barrier.

11.2  The monopolist's revenue: marginal revenue lies below price

The single fact that drives every monopoly result is this: to sell one more unit, the monopolist must lower the price — and not just on that unit, but on all the units it sells. So the extra revenue from one more cup, its marginal revenue, is the new price minus the revenue given up by marking down everything else. Marginal revenue therefore lies below the demand curve.

Marginal revenue

The change in total revenue from selling one more unit. For a price maker facing a downward-sloping demand curve, marginal revenue is always less than the price, because selling another unit requires cutting the price on every unit sold.

For a straight-line demand curve there is a clean rule: marginal revenue has the same intercept as demand but twice the slope. With \( P = 12 - 0.2Q \), marginal revenue is \( MR = 12 - 0.4Q \). Where the competitive firm of Chapter 10 saw a flat line at the market price (its marginal revenue equalled the price), the monopolist sees a marginal-revenue curve falling away beneath the demand it faces.

11.3  Profit maximization and the cost of market power

The profit-maximizing rule is the same one every firm obeys: produce the quantity where marginal revenue equals marginal cost. What differs is the price. The competitive firm's marginal revenue was the price, so it produced where price equalled marginal cost. The monopolist's marginal revenue lies below price, so at its chosen output price exceeds marginal cost — and it then reads its price up on the demand curve, above marginal revenue. Step into the decision and find the output that earns the most.

⚙ Decide first — then we will name what you did

Worked example — the monopoly outcome
Town demand is P = 12 − 0.2Q; marginal cost is a constant $4 and fixed cost is $60. Find the profit-maximizing quantity and price, the profit, and the deadweight loss.
1

That $40 is the deadweight loss of monopoly: gains from trade that simply vanish because the monopolist holds output below the efficient level to keep the price high. On the 20 cups it does not make, buyers would have valued each above the $4 it costs to produce, yet no sale happens. Notice too the transfer: raising the price from the competitive $4 to $8 hands the firm $80 that would otherwise have been consumer surplus. Consumers lose $120 in all — $80 transferred to the monopolist and $40 destroyed outright.

Deadweight loss of monopoly

The loss of total surplus caused by a monopolist producing less than the efficient quantity. Because price is set above marginal cost, mutually beneficial trades on the units between the monopoly and competitive quantities never occur, and their surplus is lost to everyone.

11.4  Natural monopoly

Sometimes a single seller is not an accident of law or resource control but a fact of cost. A natural monopoly arises when one firm can supply the whole market at a lower average cost than two or more firms could — because average total cost falls over the entire relevant range of output. This happens whenever fixed costs are enormous and the cost of serving one more customer is small: laying the network is the whole expense; pushing another unit through it is nearly free.

Natural monopoly

An industry in which a single firm can serve the entire market at lower average total cost than several firms could, because average total cost declines across the whole range of demand (large economies of scale). Marginal cost lies below average total cost throughout.

Take Mara's cart with a large fixed cost of $60 a day (rent, equipment, the licence) and a low marginal cost of $4 a cup. Average total cost is \( ATC = 60/Q + 4 \) — falling at every output as the fixed cost spreads over more cups, and always above the flat $4 marginal cost. One cart spreading that $60 over the whole town beats two carts each spreading their own $60 over half of it.

Figure 11.2 — A natural monopoly: average total cost falls throughout, marginal cost lies below it

The natural-monopoly cost shape creates a genuine dilemma. Competition is not the cure — duplicating the network would raise total costs, not lower them. But a single unregulated seller will exploit its position exactly as §11.3 showed. That tension is what regulators of utilities live inside.

11.5  Regulating monopoly

If breaking up a natural monopoly only raises costs, the alternative is to let one firm serve the market but constrain the price it may charge. Two benchmark rules pull in different directions; the interactive lets you switch between them and the unregulated outcome on the same firm.

⚙ Compare the regimes

What price should the regulator allow?

Marginal-cost pricing forces the price down to marginal cost. This is the efficient rule — it drives output to 40 cups and wipes out the deadweight loss, because the last unit is worth to buyers exactly what it costs to make. But for a natural monopoly, marginal cost lies below average total cost, so a price equal to marginal cost leaves the firm unable to cover its fixed cost: at $4 it loses the full $60. Efficient pricing here requires a permanent subsidy.

Average-cost pricing (also called fair-return pricing) sets the price where it just equals average total cost, so the firm breaks even without a subsidy. Here that price is $6, output is 30 cups, and the deadweight loss shrinks to $10 — far below the unregulated $40, though not quite zero. This is the compromise most real utility regulators reach: allow a price that covers costs plus a normal return, giving up a little efficiency to keep the firm solvent without public money.

Marginal-cost vs. average-cost pricing

Marginal-cost pricing sets P = MC: efficient (zero deadweight loss) but leaves a natural monopoly with losses, requiring a subsidy. Average-cost (fair-return) pricing sets P = ATC: the firm breaks even with no subsidy, at the cost of a small remaining deadweight loss. Real regulators — public utility commissions, rate-of-return and price-cap schemes — mostly use versions of the second.

11.6  Price discrimination

So far the monopolist has charged one price to everyone. But its real ambition is to charge each buyer the most that buyer will pay. Price discrimination is selling the same good at different prices to different buyers — not because costs differ, but because willingness to pay does. It is not always possible: it requires three conditions.

Price discrimination and its conditions

Charging different prices to different buyers for the same good, based on differences in willingness to pay. It requires (1) market power — the firm must be a price maker; (2) a way to identify or sort buyers by willingness to pay; and (3) a way to prevent resale, so low-price buyers cannot resell to high-price buyers and undercut the scheme.

Economists grade it by degree. First-degree (perfect) price discrimination charges each buyer exactly their maximum willingness to pay. Third-degree price discrimination sorts buyers into groups charged different prices — student and senior discounts, business-versus-leisure airfares, geographic pricing — charging the less price-sensitive group more. (Second-degree pricing, in between, sorts buyers by the quantity or version they choose: bulk discounts, or a basic-versus-premium tier that lets buyers reveal their own type.) Compare the two extremes on Mara's town demand.

Perfect price discrimination produces a striking result. Because the firm no longer has to cut the price on earlier units to sell one more, it keeps selling all the way to the efficient 40 cups — so output is efficient and the deadweight loss is zero. Yet consumers capture none of the gains: every scrap of surplus becomes the seller's. Market power has two faces, and here they separate cleanly — the efficiency problem of monopoly vanishes while its distributional bite is at its sharpest. Real firms cannot read minds, so perfect discrimination is a limiting case; but coupons, student fares, and personalized online prices are all imperfect grabs at the same prize.

✦ Sharma's Take — the two complaints about monopoly are not the same complaint

When people object to monopoly, they usually run two very different grievances together. One is about efficiency: the monopolist makes too little and charges too much, so trades that would benefit both sides never happen, and society is poorer by the deadweight loss. The other is about distribution: the monopolist skims surplus that would otherwise have gone to consumers. The price-discrimination interactive is worth sitting with precisely because it pries these apart. Perfect discrimination is fully efficient — not one beneficial trade is lost — and yet most people find it more objectionable than ordinary monopoly, because consumers walk away with nothing. If your intuition says "efficient but unfair," good: you have felt in your gut that efficiency and fairness are different rulers, and that a policy can score well on one while failing the other.

This is also why "monopoly" is not a synonym for "villain" in economics. A patent is a deliberately granted monopoly, and the deadweight loss it creates is the price we knowingly pay for the innovation it calls forth — a bargain if the drug would not otherwise exist. A natural monopoly's single network genuinely is the cheapest way to serve the town. The economist's job is not to be reflexively for or against market power but to weigh, case by case, the surplus lost against whatever the barrier buys us — and, where the balance tips wrong, to reach for the least clumsy remedy: regulate the price, break the barrier, or subsidize the fixed cost.

— A.S.

Monopolistic Competition

The realistic middle ground — many firms selling slightly different products, each with a little market power, yet none able to keep a profit once rivals are free to copy the idea

Perfect competition and monopoly are the two poles: no power over price, or total power. Almost nothing you actually buy comes from either. The coffee shop on your corner is not one of a thousand identical sellers — its location, its roast, and its regulars give it a following. But it is no monopoly either: a dozen other cafés compete for the same customers, and nothing stops a new one from opening next month. This is monopolistic competition, and it is the structure of most of the consumer economy — restaurants, clothing brands, apps, hair salons, breakfast cereals. It borrows the downward-sloping demand of monopoly and the free entry of perfect competition, and the collision of those two forces produces its distinctive result.

Monopolistic competition

A market with (1) many firms, (2) selling differentiated products that are close but imperfect substitutes, and (3) free entry and exit. Because its product is distinct, each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve and has a little market power; because entry is free, that power earns no lasting profit.

12.1  What makes it its own structure

Three features define it, and each matters. There are many firms, so — unlike an oligopoly — each is small enough to ignore its rivals' individual reactions and act on its own. The products are differentiated: not identical, as under perfect competition, but distinguished by quality, style, location, service, or brand, so that a price rise loses some customers but not all of them. And entry is free: no patent, resource, or scale advantage keeps newcomers out, so any profit is an open invitation to imitators. Product differentiation is the engine of the whole model.

Product differentiation

Making a product distinct from rivals' — through quality, features, style, location, service, or branding — so that buyers see them as imperfect substitutes. Differentiation is what gives each firm its own downward-sloping demand curve and thus a little power over price.

Return to Verenia. Mara's coffee cart is no longer the town's only seller, nor one of a thousand identical ones. It is one of many carts, each with its own corner, regulars, and signature blend. Raise her price a little and she loses a few customers to the cart down the street — but not all of them, because some prefer her spot or her roast. Her demand curve slopes gently downward: \( P = a - 0.15Q \), where the intercept \(a\) captures how strong her following is — and, crucially, that following weakens as more carts open.

12.2  The short run: a small monopolist

In the short run, with the number of rivals fixed, each monopolistically competitive firm looks exactly like the monopolist of Chapter 11 — only smaller and flatter. It faces a downward-sloping demand curve, so its marginal revenue lies below price, and it maximizes profit the same way: produce where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, then read the price up on demand. If that price beats average total cost, it profits; if not, it loses.

12.3  The long run: entry competes the profit away

Profit is a signal, and in a market with free entry it is a signal everyone can act on. When existing carts are earning money, new carts appear; when they are losing money, some leave. Each entrant peels away a slice of every incumbent's customers, so an incumbent's demand curve drifts left; each exit hands customers back, so demand drifts right. The drift stops only when the typical firm earns exactly zero economic profit — when its demand curve has been pushed until it just barely touches its average-total-cost curve. Watch it happen.

At rest, the demand curve is tangent to the average-total-cost curve. That single geometric fact carries the whole long-run result. Tangency means the price the firm charges exactly equals its average total cost at that quantity — so total revenue equals total cost and economic profit is zero. Any higher and demand would cut through ATC, leaving a profit that would draw entry; any lower and demand would sit entirely below ATC, forcing losses and exit. Only the tangency survives.

12.4  Excess capacity and the markup

Zero long-run profit sounds exactly like perfect competition — and in that one respect it is. But the tangency happens on the downward-sloping part of the ATC curve, not at its bottom, and that difference is the signature of monopolistic competition. Two things follow, and both are visible in the long-run diagram.

First, the firm produces less than the quantity that would minimize its average cost. In the diagram the cart settles at 10 cups, while the bottom of its ATC curve — its efficient scale — sits at 20. The 10-cup gap is excess capacity: the firm could produce more cheaply per cup if it made more, but it never does. Every café with empty tables at 3 p.m. is living this fact.

Excess capacity

The gap between a firm's efficient scale (the output that minimizes average total cost) and the smaller quantity it actually produces in long-run equilibrium. Monopolistically competitive firms always carry excess capacity, because their downward-sloping demand is tangent to ATC on its falling portion.

Second, price exceeds marginal cost. At 10 cups the price is $6.50 but the marginal cost of the last cup is only $5 — a markup of $1.50. There are customers who would happily pay, say, $5.50 for a cup that costs $5 to make, and that trade would benefit both sides, yet it never happens. As in Chapter 11, price above marginal cost means a sliver of deadweight loss.

Markup (price over marginal cost)

The amount by which price exceeds marginal cost. Any firm with a downward-sloping demand curve charges a markup; under monopolistic competition it persists even in long-run equilibrium, so the outcome is never fully efficient the way perfect competition is.

So the long-run monopolistically competitive firm sits between the two poles: it earns no profit (like perfect competition) but it prices above marginal cost and carries excess capacity (unlike perfect competition, and echoing monopoly). Whether that should trouble us is a subtler question than it first appears — and §12.5 and the closing take it up.

12.5  Differentiation, advertising, and the value of variety

The markup and the excess capacity look like waste when measured against the perfectly competitive yardstick. But that yardstick assumes every firm sells the identical product — and the entire point of monopolistic competition is that they do not. The empty tables and the price above marginal cost are the flip side of something buyers value: variety. A town with one efficient, cost-minimizing coffee cart selling one standard cup would be cheaper per cup, but poorer in choice than a town with a dozen carts each offering something a little different. The apparent inefficiency partly buys the diversity of products we enjoy.

Because differentiation is the source of each firm's power, firms spend heavily to create and advertise it — and economists have long argued about whether that spending helps or harms. The informative view holds that advertising conveys genuinely useful facts (that a product exists, what it does, where to buy it), lowering search costs and sharpening competition. The persuasive view holds that much of it merely manipulates preferences and builds brand loyalty that softens competition, burning real resources to shuffle customers between near-identical goods. Both are partly true, and which dominates varies by product. Brand names carry a related double edge: they can be a wasteful vanity, or a genuine signal of consistent quality — a firm that has invested in a reputation has something to lose by disappointing you, which is exactly why the investment reassures.

Chapter 13 · Part IV: Market Structures

Oligopoly & Game Theory

A handful of firms, each large enough that its choices move the market and provoke the others — and the reasoning tool, game theory, built to make sense of that mutual second-guessing

The three structures so far shared a convenient feature: no firm had to think about any particular rival. The price taker ignored everyone; the monopolist had no one to ignore; the monopolistic competitor faced so many rivals that no single one mattered. Oligopoly breaks that comfort. Here a few firms — sometimes just two — supply the whole market, and each is big enough that what it does visibly affects the others, who then react. Cutting your price steals their customers, so they cut theirs; the profit you imagined evaporates. Every decision is a move in a game whose other players are thinking about you while you think about them. To analyze that, economics borrows a whole toolkit: game theory.

Oligopoly

A market supplied by a small number of firms, few enough that each one's decisions noticeably affect the others — so every firm must anticipate its rivals' reactions before acting. This mutual dependence, called strategic interdependence, is what sets oligopoly apart. Examples: commercial aircraft, mobile carriers, soft drinks, gaming consoles.

13.1  Strategic interdependence

What makes oligopoly hard is exactly what makes it interesting: a firm cannot choose its best price or output in isolation, because the payoff to any choice depends on what the rivals do, and their choices depend on what they expect from it. There is no single demand curve to slide along. Instead there is a web of "if they do this, I should do that — but then they'll do this…" The tool for cutting through that regress is game theory, developed to study any situation where rational parties' fortunes are locked together.

A game has three ingredients: players (here, the firms), strategies (the choices available to each — a price, an output, whether to advertise), and payoffs (what each player gets for every combination of choices). When players choose at the same time, without seeing the other's move, we lay the payoffs out in a payoff matrix. Verenia's coffee trade has thinned to two big carts, Mara's and Tomas's, and each must set its price — High or Low — without knowing what the other will do.

13.3  The prisoner's dilemma

Here is the sting. Both firms pricing High would earn $90 each — more than the $60 they get at the equilibrium. Both would prefer the High-High cell, yet neither will choose it, because whatever the other does, undercutting pays. Rational self-interest drives them to an outcome both dislike. This is the prisoner's dilemma, the most famous structure in game theory, and it turns up wherever short-run temptation to cheat undermines a mutually beneficial deal.

Prisoner's dilemma

A game in which each player has a dominant strategy to "defect," so the Nash equilibrium has everyone defecting — even though all players would be better off if all cooperated. It shows that individually rational choices can produce a collectively poor outcome.

13.4  Oligopoly pricing, collusion, and cartels

The coffee game is oligopoly in miniature. Pricing High together is collusion — the firms behaving as one monopolist would, splitting the monopoly profit. A formal agreement to do so is a cartel. But the prisoner's dilemma explains why cartels are so fragile: at the collusive High-High outcome, each member is sitting on the exact temptation the matrix shows — cut your price a little and jump from $90 to $100 by capturing the rival's customers. Every member has that incentive at once, so the agreement tends to unravel into the competitive Low-Low outcome.

Collusion and cartels

Collusion is coordination among rival firms to raise prices or restrict output toward the monopoly outcome. A cartel is an explicit collusive agreement (OPEC is the best-known). Because each member gains by secretly cheating on the deal, cartels are inherently unstable — and, in most countries, illegal.

Notice who wins from the dilemma. The firms are trapped in the outcome they least like — but that outcome, Low-Low, is precisely the low price that consumers most like. The firms' failure to cooperate is competition, and competition is what antitrust law works to protect, by making the collusive High-High agreement illegal and hard to sustain.

13.5  Why some cartels survive: repeated games

If defection always wins, why do any cartels hold together? Because real firms do not play once and vanish — they face each other day after day. In a repeated game, a firm that cuts its price today can be punished by rivals who cut theirs tomorrow and every day after. When the game is repeated indefinitely, the lost future profit from triggering a price war can outweigh the one-time gain from cheating, so cooperation can be sustained without any formal agreement — tacit collusion. A simple strategy that supports it is tit-for-tat: match High with High, but answer any price cut with a cut of your own.

Tacit collusion

Coordination on high prices sustained without explicit agreement, through the understanding that undercutting will trigger retaliation in future rounds. It is easier to maintain with few firms, stable demand, transparent prices, and frequent interaction — and it tends to break down when any of those conditions weakens.

This is why the number of firms matters so much, and why regulators watch concentrated markets closely. With two or three firms and prices posted for all to see, tacit collusion is easy; add more firms, hide the prices, or let demand swing unpredictably, and the arrangement collapses back toward competition.

13.6  Sequential games and credible threats

Not every strategic situation is simultaneous. Often one player moves first and the other responds — a firm decides whether to enter a market, and the incumbent then decides how to react. These sequential games are drawn not as a matrix but as a game tree, and solved by backward induction: start from the last decision, work out what each player will rationally do there, and reason backward to the first move.

Figure 13.1 — A market-entry game, solved by backward induction (payoffs: Entrant, Incumbent)

Work backward. If the entrant enters, the incumbent chooses between fighting a price war (a payoff of 3) and accommodating the newcomer (5); since 5 beats 3, it accommodates. So the incumbent's threat to "fight any entrant" is not credible — it would not actually want to carry it out. Foreseeing the accommodation, the entrant compares entering (4) with staying out (0) and enters. The equilibrium is (Enter, Accommodate). The lesson generalizes: in sequential games a threat only deters if carrying it out would be in the threatener's own interest when the moment comes. Moving first can be an advantage — which is why firms sometimes commit publicly to capacity or contracts precisely to make their strategies irreversible.

✦ Sharma's Take — competition is a trap the firms keep falling into, and that is the point

Students often meet the prisoner's dilemma and feel a little sorry for the firms: they would both be richer if only they could cooperate, and the logic of the game keeps them from it. Flip that sympathy around. The very "failure" that costs the firms their $90 outcome is what delivers the low price to everyone who buys from them. Competitive markets work, when they work, not because firms want to compete — they would vastly prefer to collude — but because each firm's private incentive to undercut overpowers their shared interest in keeping prices high. The invisible hand, in an oligopoly, is really the prisoner's dilemma doing its work on the sellers' behalf against the sellers.

That reframing also tells you where to look when markets misbehave. If a concentrated industry keeps prices high year after year, the interesting question is not "why don't they compete?" — they never want to — but "what is letting them escape the dilemma?" Usually the answer is repetition plus transparency: a stable handful of firms, watching one another's prices, each able to punish a cheater tomorrow. That is why competition policy obsesses over the number of firms, over mergers that shrink it, and over anything — price-signaling, information-sharing — that helps rivals sustain the cooperation the one-shot game would have destroyed. Game theory does not just describe the market; it tells the regulator exactly which lever to reach for.

Chapter 14 · Part V: Factor Markets

Factor & Labor Markets

Turning the market diagram around — firms as buyers, households as sellers, and why the same marginal logic that priced coffee also sets wages and determines who earns what

Every market we have studied so far sells output: coffee, cars, software. But behind every cup of coffee stands a market we have not yet drawn — the one where Mara hires the people who brew it. In factor markets, the roles reverse: firms are the buyers, households are the sellers, and the "prices" are wages, rents, and interest. These markets matter enormously, because they answer one of the biggest questions in economics: who earns what, and why? Most people's income is the price their labor fetches in a factor market. The good news is that no new machinery is needed — the same marginal comparison that told Mara how many cups to brew will tell her how many workers to hire.

Factors of production and factor markets

The inputs used to produce goods and services — chiefly labor, land, and capital (machines, buildings, tools). Factor markets are where firms buy or rent these inputs from households; the wage is the price of labor, rent the price of land, and interest the price of capital.

14.1  The demand for labor is a derived demand

Nobody hires a barista for the pleasure of paying wages. Mara demands labor only because customers demand coffee — the worker is a means to the sale. Labor demand is therefore a derived demand: it inherits its strength from the product market. When the price of coffee rises, each worker's output is worth more, and Mara wants more hands; if the product market collapses, so does her hiring, at any wage.

Derived demand

Demand for a factor of production that arises from — and moves with — the demand for the product the factor helps produce. A firm's demand for baristas is derived from its customers' demand for coffee.

How much is a worker worth to Mara? Two numbers answer it. The worker's marginal product of labor (MPL) — familiar from Chapter 9 — is the extra output one more worker adds, and it diminishes as the cart gets crowded. Multiplying by the price of output converts cups into dollars: the value of the marginal product, \( VMPL = P \times MPL \), is the extra revenue one more worker brings in. At $2 a cup, a worker who adds 12 cups a day is worth $24 a day — and the next one, adding 10 cups, is worth $20.

Value of the marginal product (VMPL)

The extra revenue from employing one more unit of a factor: the factor's marginal product times the output price, \( VMPL = P \times MPL \). Because marginal product diminishes, VMPL falls as more of the factor is hired — which is why the labor demand curve slopes down.

14.2  How many workers? The hiring rule

The hiring decision is pure marginal logic: one more worker brings in VMPL and costs the wage. Take the worker if the first number is at least the second; stop otherwise. Step behind the counter and run the cart's payroll yourself.

14.3  Labor supply and the market wage

The other side of the market is us. Households supply labor by weighing the wage against the value of their time elsewhere — leisure, study, home production — and, for most purposes, a higher wage draws more hours and more people into work, so the market labor supply curve slopes up. Where city-wide demand (the sum of all firms' VMPL curves) meets supply, the equilibrium wage is set.

Figure 14.1 — The city's market for baristas: demand meets supply at w = $12, L = 100

This little diagram carries a big result. In a competitive labor market, the equilibrium wage equals the value of the marginal product of the last worker hired — workers are paid what their labor adds. Anything that raises marginal product raises wages: better tools, better technology, better training. This is the fundamental reason average wages in rich economies are many times those of a century ago — capital accumulation and technology have multiplied what an hour of work produces. And it is why a wage floor set above the equilibrium (the minimum wage of Chapter 6) creates unemployment in this diagram: at the higher wage, firms slide up the demand curve and hire fewer workers than want jobs.

14.4  Why wages differ

If wages track marginal productivity, differences in pay stop being mysterious and start being informative. Four forces do most of the work.

Human capital. Skills are produced, not born: education, training, and experience raise a worker's marginal product, and the market pays for the difference. The large and persistent earnings premium for additional schooling is the clearest pattern in labor data.

Human capital

The accumulated skills, knowledge, and experience embodied in a worker — acquired through education, training, and practice. Like physical capital, it is costly to build and raises the productivity, and hence the earnings, of its owner.

Compensating differentials. Identical skills can earn different wages when jobs differ in pleasantness. Night shifts, danger, isolation — the wage must rise to fill the unpleasant post, and the extra pay is compensation for the job itself, not the worker's productivity.

Talent scarcity and economic rent. When a factor is in fixed supply — prime land, a once-a-generation athlete — its price is driven by demand alone, and most of the payment is economic rent: money above what would be needed to keep the factor in its current use. The star would play for far less; the salary reflects scarcity, not sacrifice.

Discrimination and imperfections. Real labor markets also carry frictions the clean model omits: pay gaps not explained by productivity, employers with wage-setting power in company towns, unions bargaining collectively. Each shifts outcomes away from the competitive benchmark — which is precisely why the benchmark is useful: it tells you what part of a wage gap productivity cannot explain.

14.5  Land and capital, briefly

Labor's logic extends to the other factors with almost no editing. A hectare of land or a machine-hour is hired up to the point where the value of its marginal product equals its rental price. Because households own the factors — directly, or through savings that finance the machines — factor prices are simultaneously firms' costs and households' incomes. The theory of factor markets is, at bottom, the theory of the income distribution: each factor tends to earn the value of what it marginally contributes, so incomes track productivity, scarcity, and the accumulated human and physical capital behind each person's work.

✦ Sharma's Take — the least romantic, most useful theory of income

"Workers are paid their marginal product" strikes some students as cold and others as a defense of whatever the market currently pays. It is neither — it is a diagnostic. The theory says pay tracks what an hour of work adds, and that gives you a precise place to look whenever pay seems wrong. Is a group underpaid relative to identical productivity? Then something other than productivity — discrimination, monopsony power, immobility — is operating, and you have located a genuine failure rather than a vague grievance. Are wages in one country twenty times another's? Mostly not because anyone works twenty times harder, but because capital, technology, and institutions multiply what the same hour produces. The theory does not tell you the distribution is just; it tells you which lever moves it.

And the lever it points to most insistently is human capital. Nearly everything that durably raises a person's earnings works through one channel: raising the value of their marginal hour. That is why education pays, why experience pays, why tools pay — and why policies that build capability tend to outlast policies that only redistribute the proceeds. If you remember one sentence from this chapter, make it this: in the long run, the surest way to raise wages is to raise what an hour of work produces.

Chapter 15 · Part VI: Market Failure & Information

Externalities & Public Goods

When the deal spills over — costs and benefits that land on bystanders, why prices then tell lies, and the taxes, bargains, and institutions that make them tell the truth again

Chapter 7 delivered this book's most optimistic result: a competitive market, left alone, maximizes total surplus. But that theorem leans on a quiet assumption — that everyone affected by a trade is part of the trade. The buyer weighs the benefit, the seller weighs the cost, and the price balances them. Now let a paper mill on Verenia's river sell paper to willing customers while its effluent kills the fishing downstream. The fishermen never sat at the table. Their loss appears in no price, so the market — doing exactly what it is built to do — produces too much paper. This is an externality, the first and largest of the market failures, and this chapter is about how it distorts markets and what can be done.

Externality

A cost or benefit from an activity that falls on someone who is not a party to it — a bystander — and is therefore not reflected in the market price. Externalities can be negative (pollution, noise, congestion) or positive (vaccination, education, a neighbor's garden).

15.1  Private cost, social cost

The mill's supply curve reflects its private cost — pulp, labor, energy. But each tonne also imposes, say, $2 of harm on the river's fishermen. The social cost of a tonne is the private cost plus that $2 of external cost, so the true cost curve sits $2 above the supply curve the market sees. The market equilibrates demand with private cost; society would want demand equilibrated with social cost. The gap between those two quantities is the whole problem.

Marginal social cost (MSC)

The full cost to society of one more unit: the producer's marginal private cost (MPC) plus the marginal external cost borne by bystanders. With a negative externality, MSC > MPC, so the market — which prices only MPC — produces more than the social optimum.

15.2  The corrective tax: making prices tell the truth

If the problem is that the mill does not pay the $2 its effluent costs others, the most direct cure is to make it pay — a per-tonne tax equal to the external harm, called a Pigouvian tax after the economist who proposed it. You are the environment minister; the slider is your tax. Find the rate that fixes the market.

The corrective tax does not moralize; it prices. Once the mill pays the true cost of a tonne, its private arithmetic and society's coincide — economists say the externality has been internalized.

15.3  Positive externalities: the mirror image

Benefits can spill over just as costs do. When Tomas gets a flu shot, everyone he would have infected gains too; when a student is educated, her future neighbors, colleagues, and fellow citizens collect part of the return. Now the demand curve understates the good's value: marginal social benefit sits above private benefit, and the market produces too little. The remedy flips sign with the problem — a subsidy equal to the external benefit pulls the market up to the optimum. This is the economic case for publicly funded vaccination and schooling.

15.4  Private solutions and the Coase theorem

Government is not always required. If the mill and the fishermen could sit down costlessly, they could bargain their own way to the optimum: whoever values the marginal tonne (or the marginal fish) more would pay the other to adjust, and the haggling would stop exactly where marginal benefit meets marginal social cost. This striking result is the Coase theorem — and its fine print explains why pollution taxes still exist. The theorem needs clearly assigned property rights (who owns the river?) and low transaction costs. Two parties over a fence can bargain; ten million drivers and everyone who breathes cannot. Coasean bargaining handles the neighbor's barking dog; the atmosphere needs policy.

Coase theorem

If property rights are clearly defined and bargaining is costless, private parties will negotiate their own way to the efficient outcome, regardless of who initially holds the rights. Its practical lesson is double-edged: small-numbers externalities often fix themselves, while large-numbers ones — where bargaining is impossible — justify taxes, permits, or regulation.

Between the tax and the bargain sits a market-based hybrid: tradable pollution permits. The regulator fixes the total quantity of emissions, issues permits, and lets firms buy and sell them. Firms that can abate cheaply sell permits to firms that cannot, so the reduction happens where it costs least — the same efficiency logic as the tax, with the quantity fixed instead of the price. Cap-and-trade programs for sulfur dioxide and carbon are this idea at national scale.

15.5  Public goods and common resources

Externalities are one way markets fail; the nature of the good itself is another. Sort any good with two questions. Is it excludable — can non-payers be kept out? Is it rival — does my use leave less for you? The answers split the world into four boxes, and the market only works reliably in one of them.

A public good — non-rival and non-excludable — defeats the market from the demand side. Since you get the lighthouse's beam whether or not you paid, the selfish move is to let others pay: the free-rider problem. With everyone reasoning the same way, revenue collapses and the good goes unbuilt even when its total value far exceeds its cost. That is why defense, basic research, and streetlights are financed by taxation: the government is solving a collective-action problem, buying on behalf of everyone what no one would buy alone.

Free-rider problem

When people can enjoy a good without paying for it, each has an incentive to let others bear the cost. Private sellers then cannot cover costs, and non-excludable goods are undersupplied — the core reason public goods are publicly funded.

A common resource — rival but non-excludable — fails from the other side. Each fisherman keeps the whole value of the fish he lands but shares the cost of a thinner stock with everyone; each therefore fishes past the point that is best for all. This tragedy of the commons is a negative externality among users, and every working cure gives someone a stake in restraint: quotas, tradable catch shares, entry fees, or outright property rights. A fenced pasture is never overgrazed by its owner.

✦ Sharma's Take — the market's failures are diagnosed with the market's own tools

Notice what this chapter did not do: it never abandoned the framework. The mill's pollution, the missing lighthouse, the emptying fishery — each failure was located with the same supply-and-demand, marginal-cost-marginal-benefit apparatus that earlier chapters used to praise markets. That is the deepest point here. "Market failure" is not a slogan about capitalism; it is a precise statement that some cost or benefit is missing from a price, and the diagnosis immediately suggests the remedy — put it back in. Tax the harm, subsidize the spillover, cap and trade the emission, assign the property right. The best fixes are the least heavy-handed: they correct the price and then let the market do what it is good at.

Keep the two failure tests in your pocket, because public debate constantly confuses them with things it merely dislikes. High prices are not a market failure; scarcity is supposed to be expensive. Inequality is a serious concern, but it is a question of distribution, not of missing prices. The genuine failures — bystander effects and non-excludability — are narrower, and that narrowness is their power: they tell you exactly where intervention creates value and, just as importantly, where it does not.

Chapter 16 · Part VI: Market Failure & Information

Asymmetric Information

When one side of a deal knows what the other cannot see — how hidden quality and hidden effort can make good products vanish and whole markets unravel, and the signals, screens, and guarantees that hold them together

Every market model so far assumed that both sides knew what was being traded. Buyers knew the coffee's quality; insurers knew the driver's risk; employers knew the worker's skill. Drop that assumption and something remarkable happens: markets can fail not because a cost is missing from the price, as in Chapter 15, but because trust is missing from the trade. The seller of a used car knows whether it is sound; the buyer sees only paint. That one-sided knowledge — asymmetric information — is enough, as we are about to see, to drive every good car off the market. This chapter is about the two great information problems, hidden characteristics and hidden actions, and the ingenious devices markets have evolved to survive them.

Asymmetric information

A situation in which one party to a transaction knows something relevant that the other cannot observe. It comes in two forms: hidden characteristics known before the deal (which cars are lemons, which applicants are risky) — the source of adverse selection — and hidden actions taken after the deal (how carefully the insured drives) — the source of moral hazard.

16.1  The market for lemons

The classic case is George Akerlof's used-car market. In Verenia's car bazaar, some cars are sound and some are lemons — trouble in a shiny shell. Owners know which they have; buyers cannot tell. Suppose buyers value a good car at $8,000 and a lemon at $3,000, while owners of good cars will not part with them below $7,000 (lemon owners will take $2,000). Because a buyer cannot tell the cars apart, she will pay only what the average car is worth to her. And that innocuous fact sets a trap. Run the market yourself.

⚙ Decide first — how many lemons can this market survive?

Slide the share of lemons and watch what buyers will pay

Buyers pay the average value of the cars on offer. Good-car owners walk away if that price drops below their $7,000 floor. Increase the lemon share and find the tipping point — then watch what the market becomes on the far side of it.

The tipping point sits at 20%. Up to there, the pooled price of \( 8 - 5\lambda \) thousand dollars (where \(\lambda\) is the lemon share) still clears the good sellers' $7,000 floor, and both types trade. One lemon too many and the price dips below $7,000 — the good cars are withdrawn, buyers realize only lemons remain, and the price crashes to $3,000. The chilling part is what is lost: a good car's owner would sell at $7,500 and a buyer would happily pay it, yet that trade can no longer happen, because any car offered at $7,500 is presumed to be a lemon. This is adverse selection: the terms of trade themselves select for the worst types, and the bad drive out the good.

Adverse selection

When one side's hidden characteristics mean that the mix of who participates worsens as the terms of trade change — low prices attract only bad cars; high premiums attract only risky customers. Quality exits, the average deteriorates further, and the market can unravel entirely.

Swap cars for customers and the same unraveling stalks insurance. At any premium, coverage is most attractive to those who privately know they are likely to claim. As the risky pile in, claims rise, premiums rise, and the safest customers — overcharged at the new rate — drop out, worsening the pool again. Left alone, the spiral can shrink the market to the uninsurable; it is a key reason health insurance is so often organized around large groups, employer pools, or mandates that keep the healthy in the pool.

16.2  Moral hazard: the hidden action

Adverse selection is about what you are; moral hazard is about what you do once the contract is signed and no one is watching. The fully insured driver parks a little more carelessly; the borrower gambles a little harder with the bank's money; the salaried employee, whose pay no longer tracks each hour's effort, eases off when the manager is away. In each case, one party's action is hidden and its cost falls partly on someone else — so, exactly like an externality, too much of the careless behavior gets "produced."

Moral hazard

The tendency of a person whose actions are imperfectly monitored to act less carefully once another party bears part of the consequences — the insured taking more risk, the agent shirking on the principal. The general employer–employee, insurer–insured, lender–borrower structure is called the principal–agent problem.

Markets answer moral hazard by re-attaching consequences to actions. Insurance policies carry deductibles and co-payments so the careless bear part of every loss. Lenders demand collateral and covenants. Employers mix monitoring with incentive pay — commissions, bonuses, promotion ladders — and sometimes pay above-market "efficiency wages" precisely so the job is too valuable to risk losing by shirking. None of these devices restores full information; each simply makes the hidden action expensive enough to discourage.

16.3  Signaling: proving what you know

If the informed side has something good to hide-in-plain-sight — a sound car, real ability — it will hunt for a way to prove it. A signal is a costly action that only the good type finds worth taking, which is exactly what makes it believable. The used-car dealer offers a twelve-month warranty: cheap to promise on a sound car, ruinous on a lemon, so the promise itself separates them. A brand spends years building a reputation it would forfeit by selling one bad batch. And in the signaling view of education, a demanding degree pays partly because it is easier for the able to complete — so finishing it credibly certifies ability, over and above whatever the coursework taught. Talk is cheap; signals work because they are not.

Signaling

A costly action taken by the informed party to credibly reveal hidden quality — warranties, brand-building, certifications, education. The cost is the point: a signal separates types only if it is too expensive for the low-quality type to mimic.

16.4  Screening: designing the menu

The uninformed side is not helpless either. Screening turns the tables: design a menu of options such that different types, choosing selfishly, sort themselves. The insurer offers a high-deductible plan at a low premium beside a low-deductible plan at a high premium — drivers who privately know they are safe take the first, the risky take the second, and each has quietly disclosed what no questionnaire could extract. Employers screen with trial periods and structured interviews; lenders with credit histories; used-car buyers with the simplest screen of all, the independent mechanic's inspection.

Screening

Actions by the uninformed party to induce the informed party to reveal their type — typically a menu of contracts (deductible choices, plan tiers, probation periods) built so that each type prefers a different option and self-selects.

16.5  How much should the market's fixes reassure us?

Between signals, screens, warranties, reputations, and certifiers, real markets patch a remarkable share of their information holes — the used-car market, after all, still exists, complete with inspection reports and certified pre-owned programs. But the patches are costly (all that signaling burns real resources), and some pools — health insurance above all — are too prone to unraveling for private patches alone, which is where regulation, disclosure laws, and mandated pooling enter. As with Chapter 15's externalities, the deepest point is diagnostic: when you see an institution that looks strange — why do diplomas matter so much? why do insurers ask so many questions? why does anyone pay extra for a brand? — the odds are good that it is a repair, evolved or designed, for an information asymmetry.

✦ Sharma's Take — institutions are fossils of information problems

Once you have the lemons model, you start seeing its fingerprints everywhere, and half of the economy's odd furniture suddenly makes sense. Warranties, brand premiums, diplomas, deductibles, probation periods, licensing boards, escrow accounts, wedding rings — each is a device for making private information public or for making hidden actions costly. My advice is to treat every puzzling institution you meet as a question: what asymmetry is this solving? Asked that way, the world stops looking irrational. The peacock's tail of the labor market — the four-year degree demanded for jobs that use little of its content — is exasperating precisely because it may be an equilibrium: individually rational signaling that is collectively expensive.

And that last phrase is the chapter's sting. Unlike a Pigouvian tax, which fixes its failure cleanly, information repairs are themselves costly, and some are arms races — if everyone signals harder, the signal's bar simply rises. So the policy questions here are genuinely hard: when does mandated disclosure beat private signaling? When does pooling by law beat screening by menu? The economist's contribution is not a universal answer but the right ledger: count the trades the asymmetry destroys, count the resources the repair consumes, and choose the cheaper imperfection.

Chapter 17 · Part VII: Frontiers

Behavioral Economics

The last assumption under the microscope — what psychology reveals about how people actually choose, where the rational-actor model bends or breaks, and what a wiser economics does about it

Sixteen chapters ago this book quietly hired a protagonist: a decision-maker who weighs marginal benefit against marginal cost, treats sunk costs as sunk, discounts the future consistently, and is unmoved by how a question is worded. That character — homo economicus — has served us superbly; nearly every result so far follows from taking him seriously. Behavioral economics asks the impolite question: is he us? Its method is to import evidence from psychology — experiments, field data, brain-and-behavior research — and test the model's assumptions one by one. The findings do not demolish the framework; they map, with surprising precision, where real choosers deviate from it, and in which direction. Begin with an experiment on the only subject available: you.

Present bias is the engine of procrastination, under-saving, abandoned diets, and gym memberships used twice — and of the commitment devices people invent to bind their future selves: automatic savings deductions, deadlines, workout partners who charge no-show fees.

Present bias (time inconsistency)

Systematically overweighting immediate costs and rewards relative to all future ones, so that choices reverse as a date approaches. The standard model assumes consistent (exponential) discounting; real people discount the near future far more steeply than the far future.

17.2  Losses loom larger: prospect theory

The standard model evaluates outcomes by final wealth. Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory — the most influential single amendment behavioral economics has produced — says people instead evaluate changes relative to a reference point, and asymmetrically: a loss hurts roughly twice as much as the same-sized gain pleases. Loss aversion

The tendency for losses relative to one's reference point to weigh roughly twice as heavily as equivalent gains. It underlies the endowment effect, status-quo bias, and much of observed risk behavior — and means that how a change is framed, as a gain or a loss, can determine whether people accept it.

17.3  Judging under uncertainty: heuristics and their signatures

Computing is costly, so the mind runs on heuristics — fast rules of thumb that are usually right and predictably wrong. Three matter most for economics. Anchoring: an initial number, even an irrelevant one, drags estimates toward it — which is why the first offer in a negotiation and the "was $199" tag on a $99 jacket both work. Availability: vivid, recent, or dramatic events feel more likely than they are, so people over-fear plane crashes and under-fear the far deadlier commute. Framing: logically identical descriptions steer different choices — patients accept the operation with "90% survival" and refuse it at "10% mortality." Add the sunk cost fallacy — honoring unrecoverable spending, like sitting through a dreadful film "because the ticket was expensive" — and you have the standard model's four most reliable failure signatures. Chapter 1 told you rational choice ignores sunk costs; this chapter tells you actual choosers often do not, and predictably so.

Framing effect

A change in choices caused purely by how logically equivalent options are described — as gains versus losses, survival versus mortality, opt-in versus opt-out. Under the standard model framing should be irrelevant; its power is among the most replicated findings in the behavioral literature.

17.4  Choice architecture and nudges

If framing and defaults steer choices, then someone is always steering — there is no neutral way to present options. That observation turns the biases into a policy tool. A nudge alters the presentation of choices — the default, the order, the salience — without banning anything or changing prices. The flagship example is retirement saving: switching pension plans from opt-in to automatic enrollment (with an easy opt-out) raises participation dramatically, because the default does the work that willpower and paperwork were failing to do. Other staples: prompted organ-donor registration, calorie counts at the point of sale, redesigned forms that make the recommended option the path of least resistance.

Nudge

A deliberate design of choice architecture — defaults, ordering, salience, feedback — that predictably shifts behavior while preserving freedom to choose and leaving prices untouched. Nudges exploit the same regularities (defaults, framing, present bias) that cause the problems they treat.

Nudges deserve their scrutiny. Critics note that they can be paternalistic in soft disguise, that the line between a helpful default and manipulation is thin — the same psychology powers the "dark patterns" of subscription traps — and that effect sizes, while real, are often modest beside prices and mandates. The honest summary: choice architecture is unavoidable, so it may as well be designed consciously and transparently; but it complements, and does not replace, the classical tools.

17.5  How much survives? The defense of the standard model

It would be a poor reading of this chapter to conclude that the previous sixteen were wasted. The rational-actor model survives the behavioral evidence better than the list of biases suggests, for three reasons. Stakes and repetition discipline mistakes: people mispredict once, but shoppers, traders, and firms who err repeatedly pay repeatedly, and learn. Markets aggregate: prices can be nearly rational even when many participants are not, because arbitrage and competition punish exploitable error. And the deviations are systematic, not chaotic — which is exactly what makes them theorizable. Behavioral economics at its best is not a rival school but a refinement: the standard model as the trunk, with psychologically informed amendments grafted where the evidence demands them. Markets still clear, incentives still bite, and demand curves still slope down — drawn now for choosers with human nervous systems.

✦ Sharma's Take — the model was a mirror all along; keep both images

Students sometimes arrive at this chapter and feel a small betrayal: why did you teach me sixteen chapters of a model you knew was inexact? Because inexact and useless are different things, and because you cannot even see a bias without the rational benchmark behind it. "Present bias" is only visible against consistent discounting; the "endowment effect" is only an effect because the standard model says buying and selling prices should coincide. Behavioral economics is not the refutation of the framework — it is the framework, pointed at ourselves and read carefully. That is also how science is supposed to work: state the model sharply enough to be wrong, find where it is wrong, amend, repeat.

So here is my closing advice for the course, and it is behavioral advice. Use the rational model on the world — for predicting how markets, firms, and policies respond to incentives, it remains the best instrument ever built. Use the behavioral findings on yourself — automate your savings before present bias votes, name your sunk costs and walk away from them, notice the frame before you answer the question, and be suspicious of any deal engineered around your defaults. The rational actor is a better description of the market than of any of us alone; the discipline's deepest lesson is knowing which mirror you are looking into.
