Elasticity Explorer

Bend a demand curve with your own hands, climb the revenue hill until you find its peak, then prove you can compute an elasticity from raw market data — the same three moves Chapter 5 asks of you, made physical.

Elasticity is one number — the percentage change in quantity divided by the percentage change in price — but it decides real money: whether a price hike earns more or less, who bears a tax, why a bumper harvest can ruin farmers. This arena drills the three skills the chapter builds. Station 1 lets you reshape a demand curve and watch the number respond. Station 2 turns the total-revenue test into a hill you climb. Station 3 is the Estimator: raw before-and-after data, your midpoint arithmetic, your call. Your Estimator record saves for reporting back.

1Shape the curve

Verenia's transit authority sells a bus ride for $5, and at that fare 50,000 rides are taken each day. What nobody knows from those two numbers alone is the shape of demand around them — and the shape is everything. Drag the responsiveness slider: the curve pivots around today's market point, from cliff-steep (riders have no alternative) to nearly flat (riders scatter at the first fare hike). The test shock below the chart raises the fare by $1 and computes the midpoint elasticity of the curve you built.

⚠ What actually sets the shape

The slider stands in for the chapter's determinants: demand is more elastic when close substitutes exist, when the good is a luxury rather than a necessity, when it eats a large share of the budget, and over longer time horizons (people adapt). Every preset above is one of those forces wearing a transit costume. A steep curve is never a fact about the good alone — it is a fact about the alternatives buyers have.

2Climb the revenue hill

Back to Mara's coffee cart and the exact market from the chapter opener: demand Q = 100 − 10P. Total revenue is the rectangle under your price — price times cups — and as you slide the price, the right-hand chart traces revenue itself as a hill. The chapter's claim: the peak sits exactly where elasticity passes through 1. Find it, then test the claim by reading |E| at the top.

⚙ The total-revenue test, made physical

Where does Mara's revenue peak?

The market: your price, the quantity it buys, and the revenue rectangle
The revenue hill: total revenue at every price, with your position on it

Notice what the hill proves about a linear demand curve: elasticity is not one number for the whole curve. The same straight line is elastic up in its high-price half (left of the hill's peak going up), unit elastic at the midpoint, and inelastic down low. "This good is elastic" is always shorthand for "elastic around the current price."

✦ Sharma's Take — the most bankable number in the chapter

If microeconomics handed out one number to take into a business meeting, it would be this one. Every pricing decision — the streaming service weighing a fee hike, the airline filling seats, the city pricing its buses — is at bottom a bet on an elasticity. Get it right and the total-revenue test tells you which way to move the price before a single spreadsheet opens. It also explains the chapter's darkest little result: because food demand is inelastic, a bumper harvest that swells quantity crushes price by proportionally more, and farm revenue falls in the best growing year. Good news for eaters, ruin for growers — one number, both stories.

The working caveats: elasticity is local (Station 2 showed you the same line running from elastic to inelastic), and it grows with time as buyers find alternatives — the fare hike that sticks for six months can bleed riders over three years. Estimate it around the price you actually charge, and re-estimate as the horizon lengthens.

— A.S.
