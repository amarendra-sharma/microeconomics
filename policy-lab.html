⚠ The short side always wins

Under any binding control, the quantity that actually trades is the smaller of what buyers want and what sellers offer — the short side of the market. A ceiling leaves willing buyers unserved (shortage, queues, waiting lists); a floor leaves willing sellers unsold (surplus — and when the "good" is labor and the floor is a minimum wage, that unsold surplus is unemployment). The surplus ledger above also hides one generosity: it assumes the scarce units reach the buyers who value them most. Real rationing — by luck, queues, or connections — usually does worse, so the red triangle is a lower bound on the damage.

2The tax wedge — and who really pays

Now the subtler lever. A per-unit tax drives a wedge between what buyers pay and what sellers keep: the two prices split apart until the gap equals the tax, and the quantity shrinks to where both sides accept their share. The chapter's deepest result hides in the two responsiveness sliders: the law can say "sellers remit this tax" all it likes — the burden lands wherever it wants. Rebuild the textbook's 50/50 case, then make one side inelastic and watch the burden pile onto it.

Two regularities worth extracting before you leave this station. First, the burden shares always mirror the responsiveness ratio — the inelastic side pays, because it is the side that cannot walk away. Second, double the tax and watch the deadweight loss quadruple: the red triangle grows with the square of the tax, which is why economists prefer broad low taxes to narrow high ones.

✦ Sharma's Take — the law names the payer; the elasticities choose one

Station 2 holds the lesson I most want you to carry out of these two chapters. In 1990, the United States taxed luxury yachts, aiming squarely at wealthy buyers. But yacht buyers are the flat curve — they can buy abroad, buy later, buy a beach house instead — while shipyards and their workers are the steep one. The burden landed on the boatbuilders; thousands of jobs vanished; the tax was repealed within three years. Every incidence question yields to the same two-step you drilled here: forget who writes the check, find who cannot walk away.

And keep the ledger's other lesson beside it: every one of these levers shrinks the pie while rearranging the slices. That is not an argument for never using them — revenue must be raised, and societies may accept some deadweight loss for distributional goals. It is an argument for choosing the version that shreds least: broad and low over narrow and high, taxes on inelastic margins over elastic ones, and honest accounting of the red triangle before the vote, not after.

— A.S.
