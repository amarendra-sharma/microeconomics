Monopoly Arena

Take the only seat in town: run the ferry monopoly with your own output dial and feel MR = MC pull against P = MC; sit in the regulator's chair and discover why every fix bleeds somewhere; then watch price discrimination do something no one expects — make the monopoly less wasteful.

Chapter 11 hands one firm the whole market and asks what changes. This arena runs the chapter's exact town: demand P = 12 − 0.2Q, marginal revenue MR = 12 − 0.4Q, a constant $4 marginal cost, and a $60 fixed cost that makes average cost fall forever — a natural monopoly. The landmarks you will keep hitting: the monopolist's choice (Q = 20, P = $8, profit $20, deadweight loss $40) and the efficient outcome the market never reaches (Q = 40, P = $4). 1The monopolist's dial

In Chapter 10 the price was handed to you; now you set it — indirectly. A monopolist chooses the quantity, and the demand curve dictates what price clears it: fewer ferry crossings, higher fare. The catch is the gold MR curve below demand: to sell one more crossing you must cut the fare on every crossing, so the extra revenue is always less than the price. ⚠ The summit is at MR = MC — and that is the whole indictment

Your hill peaks at 20 crossings, where MR meets the $4 marginal cost — but at that quantity riders still value a crossing at $8, twice what one costs to provide. Every crossing from 20 to 40 is worth more to somebody than it costs, and none of them happens: that unbuilt value is the $40 deadweight loss on your cards. The monopolist is not being wicked; it is maximizing exactly as Chapter 10 taught. The waste comes from whose marginal revenue counts — the firm's, not society's.

2The regulator's chair

The ferry is also a natural monopoly: its $60 of fixed cost (the boat) spreads over every crossing, so average cost falls forever and one firm is always the cheapest way to serve the town. Splitting it up would raise costs — so the town regulates instead. You hold the rate-setting pen. Read your finished table like a menu of imperfections. Marginal-cost pricing delivers full efficiency and bankrupts the ferry ($60 a day) unless taxpayers cover the boat. Average-cost pricing keeps the firm alive without profit, at the price of a small $10 residual loss — and, the chapter warns, it dulls every incentive to cut costs, since costs are simply passed through. Leaving it alone wastes the most. Regulators do not choose between good and bad; they choose which imperfection the town can live with.

3The price-discrimination lab

Now hand the ferry a superpower: the ability to tell riders apart and charge them differently. Track the deadweight-loss card across the three clicks: $40 → $10 → $0. Discrimination lets the ferry serve riders it used to price out, so output climbs toward the efficient 40 and the waste shrinks — at the perfect limit, the monopoly is fully efficient. What changes is not the size of the pie but who eats it: consumer surplus goes $40 → $50 → $0. Student fares, senior discounts, and airline fare classes are real markets walking the same path — more people served, more surplus captured by the seller.

✦ Sharma's Take — the crime is the gap, and the gap is measurable

Popular complaints about monopoly usually aim at the wrong target: the profit. But look back at your Station 1 cards — the profit was a modest $20 while the deadweight loss was $40, twice as large. Profit is a transfer: dollars that moved from riders to the ferry, painful to riders but not destroyed. The economic crime is the gap between price and marginal cost, because every unit inside that gap is a trade worth making that never happens. That is why Station 3 startles people: price discrimination, which sounds predatory, shrinks the crime — it serves the priced-out — even as it confiscates the surplus. Efficiency and fairness ride separate rails here, and the chapter's discipline is refusing to let one masquerade as the other.

And from the regulator's chair, take the honest lesson: there was no button that produced "efficient, solvent, and incentive-compatible" at once. Real utility regulation is the art of choosing which failure to tolerate — subsidize and tax the town, break even and dull the incentives, or leave the markup and eat the waste. When you hear a debate about regulating a platform, a pipeline, or a power grid, you now own the whole menu, prices included.
