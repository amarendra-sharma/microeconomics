Profit-Max Arena

Run Mara's cart for real: feel your way up the profit hill, face the coldest decision in the book — operate at a loss or shut the cart — watch entry and exit squeeze every profit to zero, then take the Manager's Round, where you must read the diagram like a professional because the profit number stays hidden until you commit.

Chapter 10 hands the competitive firm exactly one lever — how much to produce — and one rule for pulling it: P = MC. This arena runs the chapter's exact cost structure, TC = 48 + 3.6q − 0.12q² + 0.006q³, with its two famous thresholds: the $3 shutdown floor (minimum AVC, at 10 cups) and the $6 break-even price (minimum ATC, at 20 cups). Everything below is that one formula, weaponized. The Manager's Round record saves for reporting back.

1The profit hill

Set the market price, then take the output dial in hand. The upper chart is the firm diagram you know from the chapter; the lower one plots profit itself at every output — a hill with a single summit. Climb it by hand at a few prices and notice where the summit always sits: exactly where the price line crosses the rising marginal-cost curve. That is not a coincidence to memorize; it is the hill's geometry. To the left of the crossing each extra cup earns more than it costs (the hill climbs); to the right it costs more than it earns (the hill falls).

2The shutdown decider

Now the chapter's hardest-nosed lesson. When the price falls below $6, Mara loses money no matter what she does — the question is only how much. Shutting the cart still costs the $48 rent. Operating loses whatever the best output loses. The slider pits the two losses against each other as bars: the correct choice is always the shorter bar, and the crossover sits precisely at the $3 minimum of average variable cost.

⚠ Losing money is not the same as leaving

Between $3 and $6, Mara operates while losing money, because revenue covers all the variable costs plus part of the rent she owes anyway. Below $3, every cup fails to cover even its own ingredients, and operating deepens the hole. And note what never appears in either bar's calculation: how much she paid for the cart last year. Sunk costs sat this decision out entirely — only the $48 she cannot escape and the variable costs she can.

3The long-run squeeze

Zoom out from one cart to the whole market. In the long run nobody needs permission to open a coffee cart, and nobody is chained to one. Start the market in a boom or a slump and press Advance one season repeatedly: profit lures entrants whose added supply pushes the price down; losses evict firms whose exit pushes it back up. There is exactly one price at which the churning stops.

The resting point is $6 — minimum average total cost — and it is worth pausing on how strange and wonderful that is. No regulator chose it. Free entry and exit, each cart pursuing only its own profit, ground the price down to the cheapest each cart can produce for, handed the entire efficiency gain to consumers, and left every surviving firm earning exactly zero economic profit: covering all its costs, including the owner's next-best alternative, and not a cent more.

4The Manager's Round

The graded game — and this one hides the answer. Each round deals a market price. You see the full firm diagram, but no live profit number: like a real manager, you must read the curves — find where MC meets the price, remember the $3 floor — set your output, and commit. Only then does the ledger open. Within $2 of the best possible profit counts as an optimal call. Beware: some rounds deal a price where the only right output is zero.

✦ Sharma's Take — zero profit is the strangest happy ending in economics

Students reach the end of Station 3 and feel they have witnessed a tragedy: every firm's profit ground to zero. Read it again — it is the system working perfectly. "Zero economic profit" means every cart covers its ingredients, its rent, and the full value of what its owner could earn elsewhere. Nobody is being exploited at $6; everyone is being paid exactly what their resources are worth, and consumers get coffee at the cheapest price the technology allows. The profits of the boom were never the destination — they were the signal, the flare that summoned new capacity to where society wanted it. When the flare goes dark, the message has been delivered.

And carry the Manager's Round discipline into any business you ever touch: the profit-maximizing question is never "how do we cover our costs?" or "how do we use the machine fully?" It is only ever "does the next unit bring in more than it costs?" — asked at the margin, with sunk costs banished from the room. Firms that price and produce off averages leave money on the table at every price you dealt yourself today; you now know exactly where it was left, and how to pick it up.
