Oligopoly Arena

Markets where rivals watch each other: feel the payoff matrix drag Mara and Tomas into an equilibrium neither wants, measure the exact dollar temptation to cheat a cartel, then defend cooperation across ten rounds against opponents with long memories — the bridge from this course into full game theory.

Chapter 13 is where "the market" gets faces. With two sellers instead of many, every choice is a move in a game, and this arena plays the chapter's own game: Mara and Tomas each pick High ($3) or Low ($2) prices, with the daily profits (90, 90) if both stay High, (60, 60) if both go Low, and 100 vs. 50 when one undercuts the other. ⚠ A dilemma is not a mistake

The maddening thing you just verified: Low is Tomas's best reply to either of Mara's prices, and hers to either of his — a dominant strategy for both. Two flawless reasoners therefore land on (Low, Low) and 60 each, staring across the square at the (90, 90) they both preferred. Nobody blundered. The outcome is worse for everyone because everyone played perfectly — that is what makes it a dilemma rather than an error, and why Chapter 7's invisible hand has no grip here.

2The temptation meter

Now measure the dilemma in dollars, using Chapter 11's ferry market: town demand P = 12 − 0.2Q, marginal cost $4. Mara and Tomas have formed a cartel to run it jointly like a monopoly — 20 crossings total, 10 each, at $8, for $40 apiece. Tomas honors the deal. You are Mara, alone with the schedule tonight. Every crossing you add slides the price down the residual demand left after Tomas's 10: P = 10 − 0.2q. Find your most profitable betrayal — then find out what it costs.

The retaliation button shows the ending: when both play best responses against each other, each runs about 13.3 crossings, the fare sags to about $6.67, and each earns ≈ $35.60 — forever. Five dollars once, four and a half dollars a night for good: that arithmetic is why cartels need something the one-shot game cannot supply.

✦ Sharma's Take — the shadow of the future is civilization's load-bearing wall

Station 2 gave you the dilemma at its cruelest: a guaranteed $5 tonight against $4.40 a night forever, and yet the one-shot logic says take the $5, because in a game with no tomorrow the forever never arrives. Then Station 3 showed the repair, and I want you to see how little it required — no contract, no regulator, no goodness of heart. Just an opponent who remembers, and enough future left to matter. Tit-for-Tat sustained the $90 mornings with nothing but the credible promise that betrayal would be answered. That is the deepest result in this chapter: cooperation among the self-interested is not a miracle of character; it is an equilibrium of repeated play.

And once you see it, you see it everywhere the textbook cannot follow: why reputations are worth more than any single deal, why merchants cheat tourists but not regulars, why the last month of a lease goes badly, why "we'll be doing business for years" is the strongest clause in any contract. Whenever cooperation puzzles you, find the repetition; whenever betrayal does, look for an endgame someone could see coming. This chapter is the doorway — the full house of game theory is next door, and it runs on exactly these hinges.

— A.S.
