Externality Arena

See the bill the market never opens — the cost that lands on people who never signed the deal; fix it with a tax dialed to the exact size of the harm; try fixing it with nothing but a property right and a conversation; then diagnose a dozen broken markets by their failure type.

Chapter 15 is where the invisible hand drops something. When a riverside dye works serves its customers, part of the true cost — the fouled water downstream — appears on nobody's invoice, so the market oversupplies the thing that causes it. This arena runs the chapter's market: demand P = 10 − 0.1Q, the firms' private cost MPC = 2 + 0.1Q, and a constant $2 per unit of harm to the river — so the market chooses 40 units while society's best is 30. ⚠ The optimum is not zero

Notice what your slider proved on the way down: pushing output below 30 creates deadweight loss too. Society's best point is not "no pollution" — it is the output where the last barrel's value to buyers exactly covers its full cost, harm included. Dye is genuinely useful; the river is genuinely harmed; the optimum is where those two truths balance, and Chapter 15's whole machinery exists to find that point, not to shut the factory.

2The Pigouvian dial

Now the classic repair: a per-barrel tax that makes the invisible bill visible. Every dollar of tax shifts the firms' private cost up, and the market re-solves on its own — no inspector chooses the output; prices do. Your job: find the tax that lands the market exactly on 30. There is only one right answer, and you already know what it should equal.

Two things make this tax different from every tax in Chapter 6. There, a tax on a healthy market created deadweight loss; here, a tax on a broken one destroys it — because the wedge it drives is a wedge that belonged in the prices all along. And it raises $60 while doing so: revenue collected not by distorting behavior but by correcting it, which is why economists across the political spectrum harbor a soft spot for taxing harms instead of paychecks.

3The Coase corner

Ronald Coase asked a question that rearranged the field: if the polluter and the victim can bargain cheaply, do we need the tax at all? Run the experiment. A smokehouse and a laundry share a wall. Choose the numbers, choose who holds the legal right — and watch where the bargain lands.

Run all four combinations and the pattern is the theorem: the smoke's fate never depends on who holds the right — it depends only on whether the smoke is worth more than the harm. The right decides who pays whom; bargaining steers the resource to its highest-valued use either way. The fine print is everything, though: this works only when the parties are few, identifiable, and cheap to assemble. One laundry can bargain with one smokehouse; ten thousand downstream households cannot bargain with forty factories — which is why the river needed Station 2 and the shared wall did not.

✦ Sharma's Take — the price was wrong, so fix the price

Everything in this arena is one idea wearing four costumes: markets fail when prices lie. The dye market overproduced not because anyone was greedy but because the price of dye omitted the river; the vaccine market underproduces because the price omits the neighbors you protect; the fishery empties because the price of a catch omits the fish it removes from everyone else's future. And notice the elegance of the repair you dialed in Station 2 — it does not command, inspect, or forbid. It corrects the lie in the price and then trusts the same self-interest that caused the problem to solve it. That is why a carbon price is the policy economists of nearly every persuasion converge on: not because markets failed, but because it is the most market-like repair of a market that was fed the wrong numbers.

Coase's corner adds the deepest footnote: sometimes the missing ingredient is not a tax but an owner. Many tragedies of the commons are simply externality problems where nobody holds the right that Station 3 handed out — and history's fixes (fencing the pasture, fishing quotas you can sell, tradable emission permits) are all ways of minting that missing property right. Ask of every broken market: what does the price leave out, and who, if anyone, owns the thing being damaged? The answer usually writes the policy for you.
